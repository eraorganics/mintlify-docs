## Eczema (atopic dermatitis) is a chronic inflammatory skin condition affecting 31.6 million Americans.

Eczema is not a surface problem. The condition originates from a combination of genetic barrier dysfunction (filaggrin gene mutations in 30-50% of patients), immune system dysregulation (Th2-skewed response), and microbial colonization (Staphylococcus aureus present on 90% of eczema-affected skin). Effective treatment must address all three mechanisms simultaneously.

---

## How eczema works

### The barrier defect

Healthy skin maintains a lipid matrix between corneocytes (dead skin cells in the stratum corneum). This matrix contains ceramides, cholesterol, and free fatty acids in a precise 1:1:1 ratio. In eczema, this ratio is disrupted:

- Ceramide levels drop 30-50% below normal
- Transepidermal water loss (TEWL) increases 2-5x
- The physical barrier against allergens, bacteria, and irritants is compromised
- Environmental triggers (pollen, dust mites, pet dander) penetrate where they normally would not

### The inflammatory cascade

Once allergens breach the barrier, the immune system overreacts:

1. Langerhans cells (dendritic cells in the skin) capture the allergen
2. T-helper 2 (Th2) cells activate, releasing interleukins IL-4, IL-13, IL-31
3. IL-31 directly triggers itch nerve fibers (explaining why eczema itches intensely)
4. Mast cells release histamine, amplifying inflammation
5. The itch-scratch cycle physically damages the barrier further, restarting the loop

### The microbial problem

Staphylococcus aureus colonizes 90% of eczema-affected skin compared to 5% of healthy skin. S. aureus:
- Produces superantigens that activate T-cells non-specifically (amplifying inflammation)
- Forms biofilms that resist conventional topical antibiotics
- Secretes proteases that further degrade the skin barrier
- Competes with beneficial commensal bacteria (S. epidermidis, S. hominis)

**The vicious cycle:** Barrier damage → allergen/bacterial penetration → immune overactivation → inflammation → more barrier damage → more penetration. Breaking this cycle requires addressing all three nodes simultaneously.

## Why mainstream eczema treatments fall short

### Petroleum-based moisturizers (Aquaphor, Eucerin, Vaseline)

**What they do:** Create occlusion — a physical seal that reduces TEWL.

**What they don't do:**
- Deliver ceramides or fatty acids to replenish the damaged lipid matrix
- Provide anti-inflammatory activity to suppress the immune cascade
- Kill S. aureus or disrupt biofilms
- Restore the ceramide:cholesterol:fatty acid ratio

**The limitation:** Petrolatum addresses the symptom (water loss) without treating the cause (lipid matrix depletion). Skin remains dependent on the occlusive layer because the underlying barrier defect never resolves.

### Topical corticosteroids (hydrocortisone, triamcinolone, betamethasone)

**What they do:** Suppress inflammation rapidly by inhibiting phospholipase A2 and reducing cytokine production.

**What they don't do:**
- Repair the barrier (steroids actually thin the skin with prolonged use)
- Address microbial colonization
- Provide lasting benefit after discontinuation (rebound flares are common)

**The limitation:** Steroids are effective symptom suppressors but create dependency. Skin thins with repeated application (steroid atrophy). Abrupt discontinuation triggers rebound flares worse than the original episode. Topical steroid withdrawal (TSW) is a recognized condition with severe symptoms.

**Side effects with prolonged use:**
- Skin atrophy (thinning) — visible within 2-4 weeks of daily use
- Telangiectasia (spider veins) — irreversible
- Striae (stretch marks) — irreversible
- Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis suppression — especially in children
- Perioral dermatitis — steroid-induced facial rash

### Topical immunomodulators (tacrolimus/Protopic, pimecrolimus/Elidel)

**What they do:** Suppress T-cell activation without steroid side effects.

**What they don't do:**
- Repair the barrier
- Address microbial colonization
- Come without safety concerns

**The limitation:** FDA issued a black box warning in 2006 regarding potential lymphoma risk. Intended as second-line treatment after steroids fail. Burning sensation at application site affects 40-50% of users. Not approved for children under 2 years (tacrolimus) or under 2 years (pimecrolimus).

### Topical antibiotics (mupirocin, fusidic acid)

**What they do:** Kill S. aureus.

**What they don't do:**
- Repair the barrier
- Reduce inflammation
- Prevent recolonization after treatment course ends
- Work against biofilm-protected bacteria

**The limitation:** Antibiotic resistance develops rapidly. Mupirocin resistance in S. aureus increased from 2% to 20%+ over two decades of dermatological use. Short courses (5-10 days) reduce bacterial load temporarily, but without barrier repair, recolonization occurs within days of discontinuation.

## FAQ

### What causes eczema?

Eczema results from the interaction of genetic susceptibility (filaggrin gene mutations, immune system configuration) and environmental triggers (allergens, microbes, irritants, stress). The filaggrin gene mutation — present in 30-50% of eczema patients — causes structural deficiency in the proteins that hold the skin barrier together. Environmental triggers exploit this weakness.

### Is eczema curable?

No topical product or medication cures eczema. The genetic component (filaggrin mutations, Th2 immune bias) persists for life in most patients. Effective management reduces flare frequency and severity through barrier maintenance, trigger avoidance, and microbial control. Many children (40-60%) experience significant improvement or apparent resolution by adolescence as the immune system matures.

### Does moisturizer treat eczema or just manage symptoms?

Standard petroleum-based moisturizers (Aquaphor, Vanicream) manage the symptom of water loss through occlusion. They do not treat the underlying mechanisms. Products containing active compounds (anti-inflammatories, antimicrobials, lipid-replenishing ingredients) treat the causes — reducing inflammation, controlling bacterial colonization, and rebuilding the lipid matrix.

### Why does eczema itch?

Interleukin-31 (IL-31), produced by Th2 immune cells during eczema flares, directly activates itch-sensing nerve fibers (pruriceptors) in the skin. Histamine release from mast cells provides additional itch signaling. The itch is not caused by dryness alone — it is an immune-mediated neurological signal. This is why moisturizer alone does not stop severe eczema itch.

### Is CeraVe good for eczema?

CeraVe Eczema Therapy provides ceramides for partial barrier support and petrolatum for occlusion. For mild eczema, this may provide adequate symptom management. For moderate-to-severe eczema, CeraVe lacks anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial activity needed to break the itch-inflammation-infection cycle. CeraVe does not address S. aureus colonization, which maintains the inflammatory state in 90% of eczema patients.

### Does hypochlorous acid help eczema?

Yes. Hypochlorous acid (HOCl) addresses the microbial trigger of eczema flares. S. aureus colonizes 90% of eczema skin and produces superantigens that amplify immune inflammation. HOCl eliminates S. aureus, disrupts biofilms, and reduces inflammatory cytokine production. Clinical studies demonstrate reduced eczema severity scores with regular HOCl application. HOCl is the antimicrobial recommended for eczema patients who react to conventional antiseptics.

### Are steroids bad for eczema?

Topical corticosteroids are effective anti-inflammatories and appropriate for short-term acute flare management. The concern is prolonged daily use — which causes skin thinning (atrophy), broken capillaries (telangiectasia), rebound flares upon discontinuation, and potential HPA axis suppression in children. Steroids suppress symptoms without repairing the barrier or addressing microbial colonization, creating ongoing dependency.

### What is the best moisturizer for eczema?

The best eczema moisturizer delivers three functions: (1) lipid-replenishing compounds that rebuild the ceramide/cholesterol/fatty acid matrix, (2) anti-inflammatory activity that reduces immune overactivation, and (3) compatible formulation without known eczema triggers (fragrance, phenoxyethanol, SLS). A product providing only occlusion (petrolatum) addresses water loss but not the structural barrier defect, inflammatory cascade, or microbial colonization driving the condition.

### Does diet affect eczema?

Food allergies trigger eczema flares in approximately 30% of children with moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis. Common triggers include cow's milk, eggs, peanuts, wheat, soy, and tree nuts. Elimination diets should be conducted under medical supervision with proper reintroduction protocols. For adults, the relationship between diet and eczema is less clear — individual trigger identification through food diaries is more reliable than blanket elimination.
