## What is a tree nut allergy?

A tree nut allergy is a type of allergy in which the immune system treats proteins from tree nuts as a threat. It is one branch of the wider group of food-related allergies, and it covers nuts that grow on trees — walnut, almond, cashew, pecan, hazelnut, pistachio, and others — as distinct from peanuts, which are legumes. In everyday speech people call it a walnut allergy when walnut is the trigger, or more loosely a nut sensitivity or tree-nut sensitivity. The label matters here because one of those nuts, walnut, is an ingredient in this scrub.

The reaction itself follows a familiar pattern. The body produces an IgE response to a specific allergen protein, and on later exposure that primed system reacts, sometimes mildly and sometimes severely. That is the opposite of non-allergic skin, which meets the same protein and does nothing. Because the difference between those two responses is written in a person's own immune history, no product can promise it is safe for someone with a known nut allergy — only that person and their clinician can judge that.

## Why this matters for a walnut-shell scrub

This page is deliberately direct, because the product it accompanies is not tree-nut-free. The scrub's physical exfoliant is micronized walnut shell powder, and walnut is a tree nut. That is a nut-derived ingredient, and honesty about it is more useful than reassurance. We are not going to tell you the shell is somehow exempt from the allergy; we are going to tell you plainly that if you have a walnut allergy or a broader tree nut allergy, this is a product to approach with real caution or to avoid.

A fair question is how much allergen protein sits in a processed shell powder versus the nut meat people eat. The shell is not the edible kernel, and processing changes it. But cross-reactivity and individual sensitivity make blanket claims unwise: some people react to traces, and the severity of an allergic reaction is not something to gamble on. The responsible stance is caution, not a promise. If you are allergic to walnuts, treat this scrub as one that may not be for you.

## Patch test first — or avoid

For anyone with any uncertainty about nut sensitivity, the first step is to [patch test first](/concepts/patch-test): apply a small amount to a discreet spot, wait 24 to 48 hours, and watch for redness, itching, swelling, or bumps. A patch test is a sensible screen for mild sensitivities. It is not, however, a safe way to challenge a diagnosed tree nut allergy, because a known allergy can produce a reaction that outruns a cautious spot test. So the guidance splits cleanly:

If you have a diagnosed walnut or tree-nut allergy, the safer choice is to avoid this product rather than test it, and to talk to your allergist before using anything containing a nut-derived ingredient. If you have no nut allergy but simply want to be careful, a patch test before regular use is the reasonable path. Awareness is the whole point — you cannot make a good decision about a nut-derived ingredient without first knowing it is there, which is exactly why this page exists.

### Can I use the scrub if I have a walnut allergy?

If your walnut allergy is diagnosed, the safer choice is to avoid the product and check with your allergist before using anything with a nut-derived ingredient. A patch test is not a reliable way to challenge a known allergy, because a real reaction can be more serious than a small spot test reveals.

### Is walnut shell the same as the nut you eat?

The shell is not the edible kernel, and processing it into powder changes it. Even so, cross-reactivity and individual sensitivity mean no one can promise the shell is safe for an allergic person. The prudent stance for anyone with a tree nut allergy is caution, not reassurance.

### What should I do if I am unsure whether I am sensitive?

If you have no diagnosed allergy but want to be careful, patch test a small amount on a discreet area and wait a day or two before regular use. If you have any history of nut reactions, speak with a clinician first rather than testing on your own.
