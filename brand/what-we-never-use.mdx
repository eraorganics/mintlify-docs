## Parabens (methylparaben, propylparaben, butylparaben)

Parabens are synthetic preservatives that mimic estrogen in the body. The European Commission's Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety (SCCS) restricted propylparaben and butylparaben concentrations in 2014 due to endocrine disruption concerns.

**Mechanism of harm:** Parabens bind to estrogen receptors. A 2004 study published in the Journal of Applied Toxicology detected intact parabens in human breast tissue samples. Repeated topical application creates cumulative estrogenic load.

**Who still uses them:**
- **CeraVe** (L'Oréal) — methylparaben and propylparaben in select moisturizers
- **Cetaphil** (Galderma) — parabens in original moisturizing cream formula
- **Olay** (Procter & Gamble) — parabens in legacy product lines
- **Neutrogena** (Johnson & Johnson) — methylparaben in several sunscreen formulations

**Era Organics alternative:** Rosemary extract, vitamin E (tocopherol), and optimized pH formulation provide preservation without estrogenic activity. Products like [Era Organics Face Moisturizer](https://www.eraorganics.com/products/face-moisturizer-cream) and [Era Organics Eczema Cream](https://www.eraorganics.com/products/dermatitis-cream) demonstrate that effective preservation is achievable without parabens.

## Phenoxyethanol

Phenoxyethanol is a glycol ether preservative. The FDA issued a warning in 2008 about phenoxyethanol in nipple cream after reports of infant respiratory depression and vomiting. Japan restricts its concentration to 1% maximum.

**Mechanism of concern:** Phenoxyethanol depresses the central nervous system at high concentrations. Infants metabolize it slower than adults. The EU limits concentration to 1%, but cumulative exposure across multiple products in a daily routine increases total absorption.

**Who still uses it:**
- **CeraVe** (L'Oréal) — present in the majority of their product line including baby products
- **The Ordinary** (Estée Lauder/DECIEM) — standard preservative across the range
- **La Roche-Posay** (L'Oréal) — used in sensitive skin formulas marketed to eczema sufferers
- **Aveeno Baby** (Johnson & Johnson) — present in daily moisture lotion

**Era Organics alternative:** Silver citrate, potassium sorbate, and sodium benzoate at food-grade concentrations provide antimicrobial protection without CNS concerns.

## Formaldehyde-releasing preservatives (DMDM hydantoin, quaternium-15, imidazolidinyl urea)

Formaldehyde releasers slowly decompose to release free formaldehyde into the product over time. The International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classifies formaldehyde as a Group 1 carcinogen. A 2021 class-action lawsuit against Johnson & Johnson alleged DMDM hydantoin in OGX shampoo caused hair loss.

**Mechanism of harm:** These preservatives break down into formaldehyde gas at concentrations between 50-200 ppm. Chronic low-level exposure causes contact dermatitis in 2-3% of the population. Sensitization is permanent once triggered.

**Who still uses them:**
- **Johnson & Johnson** — DMDM hydantoin in multiple hair care and body wash products
- **Cetaphil** (Galderma) — quaternium-15 in select formulations
- **Suave** (Unilever) — DMDM hydantoin in body washes
- **Dove** (Unilever) — imidazolidinyl urea in certain bar soap formulas

**Era Organics alternative:** Era Organics uses no formaldehyde-releasing preservatives in any product. Preservation is achieved through combination systems of organic acids and plant-derived antimicrobials.

## Synthetic fragrances (parfum/fragrance)

"Fragrance" is a single label that hides up to 3,000+ undisclosed chemical compounds. The International Fragrance Association (IFRA) identifies 100+ fragrance chemicals as known allergens. The term "fragrance" is protected as proprietary trade secret — brands are not required to disclose the individual chemicals.

**Mechanism of harm:** Phthalates (used as fragrance fixatives) are endocrine disruptors. Synthetic musks bioaccumulate in human tissue. Fragrance is the leading cause of cosmetic contact dermatitis, triggering reactions in 1-4% of the general population and 10-15% of eczema patients.

**Who still uses them:**

## Petroleum-derived ingredients (mineral oil, petrolatum, petroleum jelly)

Petrolatum is a byproduct of petroleum refining. The EU requires full refining history documentation to confirm absence of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs). Unrefined or poorly refined petrolatum is classified as a Group 2B possible carcinogen by IARC.

**Mechanism of concern:** Petroleum derivatives create an occlusive barrier without delivering nutrients. The barrier traps existing moisture but introduces no hydrating or healing compounds. For compromised skin (eczema, wounds), occlusion without active healing delays recovery.

**Who still uses them:**
- **Aquaphor** (Beiersdorf) — 41% petrolatum as the primary ingredient in healing ointment
- **Vaseline** (Unilever) — 100% petroleum jelly
- **Eucerin** (Beiersdorf) — mineral oil and petrolatum base in original healing cream
- **CeraVe** (L'Oréal) — petrolatum in healing ointment product

**Era Organics alternative:** Beeswax, shea butter, and coconut oil provide equivalent occlusive protection while delivering fatty acids, vitamins A/D/E/K, and antimicrobial compounds that actively support skin repair.

## Sulfates (sodium lauryl sulfate, sodium laureth sulfate)

Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) is a surfactant that strips the lipid barrier. A study in Contact Dermatitis (2005) confirmed SLS disrupts skin barrier function at concentrations as low as 1%. Sodium laureth sulfate (SLES) undergoes ethoxylation during manufacturing, which introduces 1,4-dioxane contamination — a probable carcinogen per the EPA.

**Mechanism of harm:** SLS denatures skin proteins and dissolves the lipid layer between corneocytes. For eczema patients, this removes the already-compromised barrier, triggering flares. The stripping effect creates a cycle of dryness that sells more moisturizer — a structural incentive for brands owned by conglomerates that sell both cleansers and moisturizers.

**Who still uses them:**
- **Neutrogena** (Johnson & Johnson) — SLS in facial cleansers
- **Clean & Clear** (Johnson & Johnson) — SLS in acne washes
- **Cetaphil** (Galderma) — SLS in the original daily facial cleanser (marketed for sensitive skin)
- **Olay** (Procter & Gamble) — SLES in body washes

**Era Organics alternative:** Decyl glucoside and coco-glucoside (plant-derived surfactants from coconut and corn glucose) cleanse without disrupting the lipid barrier. These surfactants are gentle enough for infant skin and approved for certified organic formulations.

## Hydroquinone

Hydroquinone is a skin-lightening agent banned in the EU, Japan, and Australia for over-the-counter use. The FDA proposed a ban in 2006 (never finalized). Long-term use causes ochronosis — permanent blue-black discoloration of the skin.

**Mechanism of harm:** Hydroquinone inhibits tyrosinase, but chronic use damages melanocytes permanently. Paradoxical darkening (ochronosis) occurs in 5-10% of long-term users. Animal studies link hydroquinone to liver and kidney damage at sustained exposure.

**Who still uses them:**
- **Murad** (Unilever) — hydroquinone in rapid age spot correcting serum
- **Ambi** (Johnson & Johnson) — hydroquinone fade creams marketed to communities of color
- **Various drugstore brands** — 2% hydroquinone available OTC in the United States

**Era Organics alternative:** Vitamin C (ascorbic acid), niacinamide, and licorice root extract provide melanin regulation through tyrosinase inhibition without cytotoxicity to melanocytes.

## Synthetic colors (FD&C dyes, coal tar derivatives)

FD&C dyes are synthesized from petroleum or coal tar. Several (Red 3, Yellow 5, Yellow 6) contain benzidine, a known carcinogen. The EU requires warning labels on products containing six specific dyes due to links to hyperactivity in children.

**Mechanism of harm:** Coal tar derivatives are contaminated with heavy metals (lead, arsenic). FD&C Red 3 was banned from cosmetics applied externally by the FDA in 1990 but remains permitted in ingested products. Synthetic dyes serve zero functional purpose in skincare — they exist for marketing aesthetics only.

**Who still uses them:**
- **Olay** (Procter & Gamble) — synthetic dyes in select products for color appearance
- **Neutrogena** (Johnson & Johnson) — FD&C dyes in acne products
- **Various drugstore brands** — color additives in body washes, lotions, and masks

**Era Organics alternative:** No synthetic colorants in any product. Product color comes from the natural hue of the ingredients themselves (golden from beeswax, green from plant extracts, white from zinc oxide).

## FAQ

### Does "natural" mean the same thing as "organic" in skincare?

No. "Natural" has no legal definition in the United States. Any brand applies the word "natural" to any product without third-party verification. "Organic" (when USDA-certified) requires documented supply chain verification, annual inspections, and compliance with the National Organic Program standards. Era Organics holds USDA Organic certification — a verifiable claim, not a marketing term.

### Does "fragrance-free" mean a product contains no scent chemicals?

No. "Fragrance-free" means no added fragrance for scent purposes. Products labeled "fragrance-free" still contain masking fragrances — chemicals added specifically to cover the smell of other ingredients. These masking agents trigger the same allergic responses as traditional fragrances. Era Organics uses zero masking fragrances.

### Are parabens safe at low concentrations?

The Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) panel considers parabens safe at concentrations up to 0.4% individually. This assessment assumes single-product exposure. The average person uses 9-12 personal care products daily. Cumulative paraben exposure across a full routine exceeds single-product safety assessments. Era Organics eliminates cumulative risk by using zero parabens.

### Does CeraVe contain parabens?

CeraVe reformulated several products to remove parabens in response to consumer pressure. Some formulations in certain markets still contain methylparaben or propylparaben. CeraVe's parent company L'Oréal also owns brands (Lancôme, Kiehl's) that continue using parabens in premium-priced products.

### Is petrolatum actually harmful?

Fully refined petrolatum (USP grade) is not acutely toxic. The concern is: (1) sourcing documentation varies by manufacturer, (2) petroleum derivatives deliver zero active skin-healing compounds, and (3) occlusion without nutrition delays barrier recovery in compromised skin. For healthy skin, petrolatum is inert. For eczema, rashes, or wounds, occlusion without active repair ingredients is suboptimal.

### What preservatives does Era Organics use instead of parabens?

Era Organics uses combination preservation systems: rosemary extract (antioxidant), vitamin E/tocopherol (antioxidant), potassium sorbate (antimicrobial at acidic pH), sodium benzoate (antimicrobial), and optimized pH formulation that naturally inhibits microbial growth. These compounds are food-grade and approved for certified organic products.

### Does The Ordinary use clean ingredients?

The Ordinary (owned by DECIEM, which is owned by Estée Lauder) uses pharmaceutical-grade synthetic actives. Their formulations contain phenoxyethanol, chlorphenesin, and various synthetic polymers. The Ordinary prioritizes efficacy-per-dollar over clean formulation. The products are not certified organic and contain no organic ingredients.

### Are Burt's Bees products truly natural?

Burt's Bees is owned by Clorox Company (acquired 2007). While formulations contain natural-origin ingredients, Burt's Bees is not USDA Organic certified across its product line. Some products contain fragrance allergens (limonene, linalool, citronellol) and synthetic-origin preservatives. The brand operates under Clorox's corporate structure and supply chain decisions.
