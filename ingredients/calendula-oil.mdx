## What is Calendula Oil in Skincare?

Calendula oil in skincare is a botanical extract derived from the flowers of *Calendula officinalis*, the common pot marigold, prized for its skin-soothing and barrier-supporting properties. The oil carries flavonoids and carotenoids from the calendula oil plant's petals, which calm redness and soften dry or irritated skin on contact. Calendula officinalis flower oil is produced by macerating dried marigold flowers in a carrier oil until the active constituents transfer into the oil phase — a process that distinguishes it from steam-distilled essential oils.

Calendula infused oil draws particular attention in wound-healing research. Calendula officinalis flower extract accelerated wound closure to 90.0% on day eight of treatment, compared with 51.1% in the untreated control group, and shortened re-epithelization from 17.7 days to as few as 13 days at a 100 mg/kg dose (Preethi K et al., Journal of Basic and Clinical Physiology and Pharmacology, 2009). Those findings position calendula among the [soothing botanical skincare oils](/ingredients) with documented skin-repair activity.

Calendula is a member of the Compositae (Asteraceae) family, and skin-sensitized individuals warrant a patch test before first use. Patch testing identified a contact reaction rate of approximately 2.03% for marigold extract in a study population (Reider N et al., Contact Dermatitis, 2001). For most users, calendula oil moisturizes and calms without irritation, making it a practical ingredient for formulas addressing diaper rash, dermatitis, and general dryness.

## How Does Calendula Oil Work on Skin?

Calendula oil mechanism centers on three classes of bioactive compounds, flavonoids, triterpenoids, and carotenoids, extracted from the flowers of *Calendula officinalis* through cold-pressing or oil infusion. Triterpenoids bind to inflammatory pathways in the outer skin layers, while carotenoids absorb free radicals at the surface. Flavonoids support the connective tissue proteins that hold the skin barrier together.

Calendula oil for skin barrier function operates through its fatty-acid profile, which is rich in linoleic acid and oleic acid. Calendula Oil's lipids intercalate into the outer skin layer (stratum corneum) (the outermost skin layer), reducing moisture loss through the skin (transepidermal water loss) and restoring the lipid matrix where dryness or irritation has degraded it. The result is a measurably softer, less reactive surface without occlusion.

How calendula oil works at a molecular level is clarified by extraction research: chlorogenic acid, caffeic acid, and ferulic acid concentrate at rates 40–70% greater than in the original plant extract when processed under supercritical conditions (Mur et al., *Antioxidants*, 2021). Calendula Oil's phenolic acids are the compounds most associated with calendula's soothing and antioxidant activity on irritated skin. Higher concentration of these acids in a finished oil translates to greater protective activity per gram applied.

The cross-linking behavior of *Calendula officinalis* extract with collagen-based scaffolds further illustrates how the oil interacts with skin tissue. In vitro collagenase digestion data showed that associating *C. Officinalis* extract with a scaffold significantly increases structural resistance to enzymatic degradation (Millán et al., *Drug Delivery and Translational Research*, 2016). Topically, this suggests calendula compounds stabilize the extracellular matrix proteins that maintain skin integrity under stress.

## Is Calendula Oil Safe, and Which Skin Types is it For?

Yes, calendula oil is safe for topical use across a wide range of skin types. The International Journal of Toxicology's safety assessment of Calendula officinalis extract concluded that calendula oil is safe as used in cosmetic formulations (International Journal of Toxicology, 2001). Sensitive and infant skin all tolerate calendula oil well, given its mild fatty-acid profile and low irritation potential.

Calendula oil skin types span from oily and combination to severely compromised and reactive. The oil's triterpenoids and flavonoids calm redness without clogging pores, and kaolin clay in Era Organics absorbs excess sebum alongside the oil's soothing action. Newborn and toddler skin respond particularly well, which is why the balm is formulated for diaper rash and general infant skin comfort.

One meaningful safety note: calendula ragweed allergy is a real cross-reactivity concern. Calendula officinalis belongs to the Compositae (daisy) family, the same botanical family as ragweed. A contact dermatitis case report documented skin deterioration traced to Compositae allergens, with rapid improvement after those allergens were removed (Wintzen M et al., Contact Dermatitis, 2003). Anyone with a confirmed ragweed or Compositae sensitivity should patch test on a small area for 24 hours before full application.

For everyone outside that allergy profile, calendula oil is a well-tolerated cosmetic ingredient with decades of safe topical use behind it. Patch test before first use regardless — standard guidance for any new topical product.
