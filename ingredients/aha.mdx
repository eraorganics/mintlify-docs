## What is an AHA?

An AHA, or alpha hydroxy acid, is a water-soluble organic acid used to dissolve the bonds between cells on the skin's surface. Chemically, an AHA is a type of organic acid, and in skincare it works as a chemical exfoliant — one that loosens dead cells by reaction rather than by scrubbing. You will see it written as alpha hydroxy acid, described as a fruit acid because many are derived from fruits and milk, or listed simply as an AHA exfoliant, and all three refer to the same family of surface-acting acids. Because AHAs are water-soluble, they concentrate their work on the outer skin rather than deep inside the pore, which shapes what they are good at.

The family has several members. Glycolic acid, the smallest and most widely used, penetrates readily; lactic acid is a little larger and often felt as gentler; and mandelic acid is larger still, which slows it down and makes it a milder option. Used regularly, AHAs are valued for the brightness and smoother, more even look they can lend the skin over time — the reason they carry a reputation for renewal and anti-aging appeal. They are a legitimate and popular way to exfoliate, and understanding them helps put a physical or enzyme scrub in context rather than in competition.

## Concentration, pH, and water solubility

Three attributes govern how an AHA behaves: its concentration, the pH of the formula, and its water solubility. Concentration is how much acid is present; higher percentages act more strongly. The pH matters just as much, because AHAs are more active at a lower, more acidic pH — the same concentration can feel mild or potent depending on it. Water solubility is the defining trait that separates this family from the oil-loving acids: because an AHA dissolves in water, it spreads across the moist surface of the skin and works on the outer layer, which is why it is prized for surface brightness and texture.

That water-loving nature is exactly the contrast with a BHA. Where an AHA stays on the surface, a [beta hydroxy acid](/ingredients/bha) is oil-soluble and can travel into the oily lining of a pore, so the two acids suit different goals. And both of them differ again from a physical abrasive, which removes cells by friction with no chemistry at all. None of these is universally best; they are simply different routes to the same outcome of a clearer, smoother-looking surface. A strong AHA belongs to the world of the chemical peel, while a gentle scrub sits at the other end of the shelf.

## Frequently asked questions about AHA

### What is the difference between an AHA and a face scrub?

An AHA dissolves the bonds between dead surface cells with a water-soluble acid, while a face scrub lifts those cells away by friction from fine particles. One is chemical exfoliation, the other physical. They reach the same dead-cell layer by different routes, and each suits different preferences and skin.

### What is the difference between an AHA and a BHA?

An AHA is water-soluble and works on the skin's surface, while a BHA is oil-soluble and can penetrate the oily lining of a pore. That makes AHAs a common choice for surface smoothness and even tone, and BHAs a common choice for oil-prone, congested skin. Many routines use one or the other depending on their goal.

### Can I use an AHA and a scrub together?

Not in the same session — both exfoliate, and stacking them can over-work the skin. Keep an acid and a physical scrub on separate days, and moisturize after either. Alternating a gentle scrub with an occasional acid lets each renew the surface without doing too much at once.

### Which AHA is the gentlest?

Larger AHA molecules tend to act more mildly. Mandelic acid is the largest common member and is often considered the gentlest, lactic acid sits in the middle, and glycolic acid is the smallest and most active. Concentration and pH also shape how strong any AHA feels on the skin.
