## What is a BHA?

A BHA, or beta hydroxy acid, is an oil-soluble organic acid used to exfoliate the skin, including inside the lining of a pore. Like its water-loving cousin the AHA, a BHA is a type of organic acid that works as a chemical exfoliant — loosening dead cells by reaction rather than by scrubbing — but its defining trait is that it dissolves in oil. You will see it written as a beta hydroxy acid or listed simply as a BHA exfoliant, and both names describe the same oil-soluble family. The best-known member by far is salicylic acid, which is why in everyday skincare talk "BHA" and salicylic acid are often used to mean the same thing.

That oil solubility is what gives a BHA its particular character. Because it mixes with oil, a BHA can travel past the surface into the sebum inside a pore and exfoliate there, which is why it is associated with a deep-cleanse, pore-clearing reputation and is a common choice for oil-prone skin. It is a genuinely useful and popular ingredient, and understanding it helps frame where a gentle physical-and-enzyme scrub fits. The two are not rivals so much as tools suited to different goals.

## Oil solubility, concentration, and pore penetration

Three attributes define how a BHA behaves: its oil solubility, its concentration, and its ability to reach into the pore. Oil solubility comes down to the molecule's lipophilic structure — its oil-loving shape — which lets it dissolve into sebum rather than sitting on the wet surface the way an AHA does. Concentration then sets the strength; leave-on BHA products are usually formulated in a low range so they can be used without over-doing it. Pore penetration is the consequence of the first two: because the acid mixes with oil, it can work inside the oily channel of a pore, exactly where surface-only methods cannot reach.

This is the clean contrast with an AHA and with a physical abrasive. An [alpha hydroxy acid](/ingredients/aha) is water-soluble and stays on the surface, better suited to overall smoothness and even tone; a physical abrasive removes cells by friction with no chemistry at all. A BHA sits apart from both by going into the pore lining. The most common BHA, covered in more detail on the [salicylic acid for blackheads](/ingredients/salicylic-acid) reference, is typically used in low concentrations for that oil-dissolving, pore-clearing role. Each of these acids and methods is a different route to clearer, smoother-looking skin, and none is universally best.

## How a BHA compares with a gentle scrub

A BHA and a face scrub both exfoliate, but by different mechanisms and toward different goals. A BHA dissolves and works inside the oily pore lining; a scrub buffs the loose dead-cell film off the surface by friction. For oil-prone, congested-looking skin, a BHA has an obvious appeal because it reaches where friction cannot. For everyday smoothing and polishing, a gentle scrub is the simpler habit. ## Frequently asked questions about BHA

### What is the difference between a BHA and an AHA?

A BHA is oil-soluble and can penetrate the oily lining of a pore, while an AHA is water-soluble and works on the skin's surface. That makes BHAs a common choice for oil-prone, congested-looking skin and AHAs a common choice for overall surface smoothness and even tone. Neither is universally better; they suit different goals.

### Is salicylic acid a BHA?

Yes. Salicylic acid is the most widely used beta hydroxy acid, so the terms are often used interchangeably. It is the oil-soluble acid behind most BHA products, usually formulated in low concentrations. The salicylic acid reference covers its properties and typical use in more detail.

### Can I use a BHA and a face scrub together?

Not in the same session — both exfoliate, and combining them can over-work the skin. Keep an acid and a physical scrub on separate days, and moisturize after either. Alternating a gentle scrub with an occasional acid lets each renew the surface without doing too much at once.

### Is a BHA better than a scrub for oily skin?

They do different things. A BHA is oil-soluble and can work inside the pore, which suits oil-prone skin, while a scrub buffs the surface by friction. Some routines use a gentle scrub for everyday smoothing and reserve a BHA for pore-focused days. The right choice depends on your skin and your goal.
