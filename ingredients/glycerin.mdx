## What it is

Glycerin (glycerol) is a simple polyol compound with three hydroxyl groups, a molecular weight of 92.09 g/mol, and the chemical formula C₃H₈O₃. Glycerin exists as a colorless, odorless, viscous liquid that is completely water-soluble and hygroscopic — meaning it actively draws moisture from the surrounding environment. The compound occurs naturally in all animal and vegetable fats as the backbone of triglyceride molecules and constitutes approximately 10% of human skin lipids.

Glycerin ranks as the most widely used humectant in cosmetic formulations globally. The Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR) Expert Panel has repeatedly confirmed glycerin's safety at concentrations up to 99% with no irritation, sensitization, or systemic toxicity.

---

## How it works

Glycerin draws water from two sources: the atmosphere (when humidity exceeds 65%) and the deeper dermal layers of skin. The three hydroxyl groups form hydrogen bonds with water molecules, creating a hydration reservoir within the stratum corneum that persists for 8-12 hours after application. Glycerin increases stratum corneum hydration by 20-40% within 30 minutes of application at concentrations as low as 3%.

Glycerin penetrates into the stratum corneum rather than sitting on the surface, integrating into the lipid matrix between corneocytes. This integration improves skin barrier function by maintaining optimal water content in the intercellular lipid bilayers — dry lipid layers crack and separate, compromising barrier integrity.

Glycerin also acts as a signal molecule that regulates keratinocyte maturation. Adequate glycerin levels in the stratum corneum trigger production of aquaporin-3 (AQP3), a water/glycerol channel protein that facilitates moisture transport between skin layers. AQP3 expression decreases with age and in dry skin conditions — topical glycerin restores this transport mechanism.

Glycerin accelerates wound healing by maintaining optimal moisture levels at the wound bed. Cells migrate faster across hydrated surfaces compared to dry environments, and glycerin prevents the eschar (scab) formation that delays epithelial closure.

## What the research says

A double-blind randomized controlled trial found that glycerin-based moisturizers increased skin hydration by 31% at 2 weeks and improved barrier function (reduced TEWL by 15%) compared to glycerin-free control formulations (Lodén et al., "The increase in skin hydration after application of emollients with different amounts of lipids," Acta Dermato-Venereologica, 2001) [SOURCE NEEDED].

Glycerin at 20% concentration demonstrated superior moisturization to hyaluronic acid at 0.1% in a comparative study measuring corneometer values over 24 hours (Fluhr et al., "Glycerol and the skin: holistic approach to its origin and functions," British Journal of Dermatology, 2008).

A 2003 study showed glycerin increased aquaporin-3 expression in human keratinocytes by 40% within 24 hours of exposure, establishing the molecular mechanism for glycerin's long-term hydration benefits (Hara et al., "Glycerol replacement corrects defective skin hydration, elasticity, and barrier function in aquaporin-3-deficient mice," PNAS, 2003).

Glycerin reduced irritation from sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS) by 50% when co-applied, demonstrating protective effects against surfactant-induced barrier damage (Bettinger et al., "The influence of glycerol on the barrier properties of stratum corneum," British Journal of Dermatology, 1998) [SOURCE NEEDED].

## Who benefits

Glycerin benefits all skin types requiring hydration — which encompasses virtually everyone. Dry skin types benefit from glycerin's water-drawing properties that address dehydration at the cellular level rather than merely coating the surface. Oily skin types benefit from lightweight, non-comedogenic hydration that provides moisture without contributing additional oil.

Glycerin benefits individuals with compromised skin barriers (eczema, psoriasis, post-procedure skin) by restoring optimal water content to damaged intercellular lipids. Mature skin benefits from glycerin's stimulation of aquaporin-3 channels that decline with age.

Glycerin suits every climate. In humid environments (above 65% relative humidity), glycerin draws atmospheric moisture into the skin. In dry environments, glycerin draws water from deeper dermal layers — requiring combination with occlusive ingredients to prevent net moisture loss from skin.

---

## What to look for

Concentration between 3% and 20% delivers optimal hydration without the tacky feel that occurs above 25%. Most well-formulated moisturizers contain glycerin at 5-15%. Products listing glycerin within the first 5 ingredients on the INCI list contain functional concentrations.

Vegetable-derived glycerin (from soybean, coconut, or palm oil) is chemically identical to synthetic glycerin but carries the "natural" sourcing preferred for clean beauty positioning. USP-grade (99.5%+ purity) ensures absence of residual methanol or heavy metals from manufacturing.

Glycerin combined with occlusive ingredients (ceramides, squalane, shea butter) creates a complete moisturization system: glycerin draws water in, occlusives prevent water from escaping. Glycerin alone without occlusive backup in dry climates draws water from deeper skin layers without replenishment.

---

## What to avoid

Pure glycerin applied undiluted to skin in dry climates (below 40% humidity) pulls water from the dermis faster than ambient humidity replenishes — potentially creating net dehydration. Glycerin requires dilution to 3-20% and combination with occlusives for dry climate use.

Synthetic glycerin from petrochemical sources (epichlorohydrin process) contains trace contaminants including monochloropropanediol (MCPD) — a potential carcinogen. Vegetable-sourced or properly purified USP-grade glycerin avoids this contamination.

Products marketing "glycerin-free" formulations as superior misunderstand the ingredient's function. Glycerin is one of the most studied, safest, and most effective humectants available. Its absence indicates either reformulation with more expensive alternatives or removal based on marketing trends rather than science.

## How competitors use it

Glycerin appears in over 80% of commercial moisturizers because its efficacy, safety, and cost make it the obvious humectant choice. CeraVe, Cetaphil, and Vanicream all use glycerin as their primary humectant at 5-15% concentrations.

## FAQ

**What does glycerin do for skin?**
Glycerin draws water from the atmosphere and deeper skin layers into the stratum corneum, increasing skin hydration by 20-40% within 30 minutes. Glycerin also stimulates aquaporin-3 water channels and improves skin barrier function.

**Is glycerin good for oily skin?**
Glycerin provides lightweight, non-comedogenic hydration without adding oil to the skin. Dehydrated oily skin often overproduces sebum to compensate for water loss — glycerin addresses the underlying dehydration that triggers excess oil production.

**Does glycerin clog pores?**
Glycerin has a comedogenic rating of zero. The molecule is water-soluble, does not accumulate in pores, and does not contribute to acne formation. Glycerin is safe for acne-prone skin at all studied concentrations.

**Is vegetable glycerin better than synthetic?**
Vegetable and synthetic glycerin are chemically identical molecules. The difference lies in potential trace contaminants from manufacturing — USP-grade vegetable glycerin avoids the monochloropropanediol contamination possible in petrochemical-derived glycerin.

**How much glycerin should a moisturizer contain?**
Optimal concentration ranges from 3% to 20%. Products listing glycerin in the first 5 ingredients contain functional amounts. Below 3% provides negligible hydration benefit. Above 25% creates a sticky texture without proportional efficacy gains.

**Does glycerin work in dry climates?**
Glycerin in dry climates (below 40% humidity) draws water from deeper skin layers rather than the atmosphere. Combination with occlusive ingredients (ceramides, oils, shea butter) prevents this moisture from evaporating and maintains net positive hydration.

**Is glycerin the same as glycerol?**
Glycerin and glycerol refer to the same molecule (C₃H₈O₃). "Glycerol" is the IUPAC chemical name. "Glycerin" is the common name used in cosmetic labeling and everyday language. No functional or structural difference exists.

**How does glycerin compare to hyaluronic acid?**
Glycerin at 20% outperforms hyaluronic acid at 0.1% in sustained hydration studies measuring corneometer values over 24 hours. Glycerin penetrates the stratum corneum while high-molecular-weight HA remains on the surface. Both ingredients complement each other in well-formulated products.
