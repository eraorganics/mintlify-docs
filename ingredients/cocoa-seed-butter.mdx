Cocoa seed butter (the fat extracted from the roasted seeds of *Theobroma cacao*) is a dense occlusive emollient that forms a protective layer over the skin surface, slowing moisture loss through the skin (transepidermal water loss). Skin that loses moisture too rapidly, the central problem in eczema-prone and newborn skin, stays softer and more resilient when that evaporative pathway is slowed. Cocoa seed butter supplies that barrier function through its high concentration of stearic and oleic fatty acids, which mimic the lipid composition of the outermost skin layer.

Dry, flaky patches on a baby's scalp, cheeks, or diaper area often reflect a compromised outer barrier rather than a shortage of water inside the skin. Cocoa seed butter addresses that physical gap by sitting on the outer skin layer (stratum corneum) and physically reducing moisture escape. Superbalm pairs cocoa seed butter with Beeswax, which reinforces the same occlusive layer, creating a double barrier that protects fragile or chafed skin against environmental stressors including wind, cold, and friction.

Fatty acid composition matters for skin tolerance, particularly in newborns whose barriers are still adapting. Stearic acid, dominant in cocoa seed butter, is a saturated long-chain fatty acid that integrates into the intercellular lipid matrix without disrupting the skin's natural pH balance. Era Organics Superbalm healing ointment combines this with Sunflower Oil, whose linoleic acid content supports barrier lipid restoration in compromised skin.

Danby et al. (*Pediatric Dermatology*, 2013) examined how topical oil composition affects adult skin barrier function by having one cohort apply six drops of olive oil to one forearm twice daily for 5 weeks and a second cohort apply six drops of olive oil to one forearm and six drops of sunflower seed oil to the other twice daily for 4 weeks, noting that fatty acid profile drives whether an oil supports or strains the barrier — a principle that applies equally to infant skin exposed to repeated wetting and drying in the diaper area (Danby et al., *Pediatric Dermatology*, 2013).

Cocoa seed butter's texture gives Superbalm its smooth, spreadable body. The balm glides over raw or irritated skin without requiring friction-heavy rubbing, which matters when applying product to a baby with eczema flares or cradle cap where mechanical aggravation worsens redness. A dime- to quarter-sized amount covers large areas of dry skin efficiently, and the ointment base locks the other active botanical ingredients, Calendula Oil, Chamomile, Avocado Oil, in sustained contact with the skin surface rather than letting them evaporate quickly.

Patch test the healing ointment on the inside of the elbow before first use, especially for users with known Asteraceae sensitivities or tree-nut allergies, as cocoa is classified as a tree nut by some regulatory frameworks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is cocoa butter good for your skin?

Cocoa butter (properly called cocoa seed butter) is a dense occlusive emollient, meaning it works by forming a physical layer over the skin surface that slows moisture from escaping rather than adding water directly. That mechanism matters most when the outer skin barrier is compromised — as it is in eczema and atopic dermatitis — because slowing that moisture loss keeps skin softer and more resilient.

Its fatty acid profile, dominated by stearic and oleic acids, closely mimics the lipids naturally found in the outermost skin layer, which means it integrates without disrupting the skin's pH balance.

### Is cocoa butter safe or toxic?

Cocoa seed butter — the fat pressed from roasted Theobroma cacao seeds — is safe for most people and widely used in skincare as an occlusive emollient. Rather than penetrating deep into skin, it sits on the surface and forms a physical barrier that slows transepidermal water loss, keeping dry or barrier-compromised skin softer and more resilient.

Its high concentration of stearic and oleic fatty acids closely mirrors the lipid composition of the skin's outermost layer (the stratum corneum), helping it integrate smoothly without disrupting the skin's natural pH balance.
