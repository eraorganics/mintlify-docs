## What is skin barrier function?

Skin barrier function is a physiological process — the outermost skin layer's ability to protect the body from irritants and hold moisture in. It travels under a few names: epidermal barrier function, skin barrier integrity, or just the skin barrier. When that process falters, the result is a compromised skin barrier or an impaired skin barrier, terms for the same surface losing its ability to keep threats out and water in. The outer layer, the stratum corneum, does most of this work. Its slightly acidic surface, the acid mantle, is a key part of the defense. The barrier's acidity is not incidental. A study on pH and infection explained that an [acidic stratum corneum pH creates a hostile environment for colonization by pathogens](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30130777/). Protect that acidity, and the barrier defends better.

## How the skin barrier is measured

Skin barrier integrity is not a vague idea; it is measured through three attributes. The first is transepidermal water loss, or TEWL, recorded in g/m²/h — the rate at which water escapes through the surface. A low TEWL signals an intact barrier; a high one points to a compromised skin barrier leaking moisture. The second is surface pH, which sits in a healthy range of about 4.5 to 6.5. Drift above that band toward alkaline weakens the process. The third is the lipid bilayer thickness in stratum corneum, roughly 10 to 40 nm of stacked fats that mortar the surface cells together. Read together, TEWL, pH level, and lipid bilayer thickness in stratum corneum describe whether epidermal barrier function is holding or slipping. Anything that raises TEWL, pushes pH alkaline, or thins the lipid bilayer moves the skin toward an impaired skin barrier.

## How the acid mantle protects skin

The acid mantle does two jobs. It blocks microbes, and it helps the barrier build itself — the skin's own way of enhancing skin barrier function from the inside. A 2021 study found that an [acidic pH is required for the proper assembly of skin barrier lipids](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33675786/). That assembly is how the body naturally works to restore skin barrier function after minor disruption, and it depends on keeping the surface pH in that 4.5 to 6.5 window.

Acidity also carries antibacterial weight. A 2023 study linked the [skin's acid mantle and natural moisturizing factors to antibacterial activity against Staphylococcus aureus](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37378303/). ### What does a compromised skin barrier look like?

A compromised skin barrier shows higher transepidermal water loss, a surface pH pushed above the healthy 4.5 to 6.5 range, and thinned lipids in the stratum corneum. The skin feels dry, tight, and reactive because epidermal barrier function is no longer holding moisture in or irritants out.

### Why does skin pH matter?

An acidic surface keeps pathogens from colonizing and helps the barrier lipids assemble correctly. Alkaline products push the skin the wrong way. Keeping the surface gentle protects both jobs.
