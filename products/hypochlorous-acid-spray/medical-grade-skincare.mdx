## What is medical-grade skincare?

Medical-grade skincare is a marketing term for products positioned as higher-strength or clinic-quality. It has no single legal definition, so what it means depends on the brand using it. Usually it points to a purer active, a clinically-studied ingredient, or a formula used in medical settings. [Era Organics](https://share.google/HYf2096Y9RKbQFVOx) describes the hypochlorous acid in Happy Skin Spray as medical-grade because the molecule has a genuine clinical footprint.

That footprint is real. A 2025 review documented hypochlorous acid's [use across dermatology, surgery, dentistry, and other specialties](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41462936/). The same molecule sits behind clinical and cosmetic products alike.

## What makes hypochlorous acid clinic-relevant

The link to medical use is the molecule, not the label. Hypochlorous acid is applied in wound care and procedure recovery because it is effective and gentle. A 2020 review called stabilized HOCl a potential [gold standard for wound care and scar management](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31904191/) in surgical settings.

A cosmetic spray borrows that pedigree by using the same active. Era Organics' spray delivers hypochlorous acid at 0.02% (200 ppm) — a low cosmetic concentration, not a surgical one. The mechanism is covered in [antimicrobial activity for skin](/products/hypochlorous-acid-spray/antimicrobial-activity).

## Reading the label honestly

The honest version is simple. Medical-grade signals a clinically-used active in a clean formula; it is not a regulatory rank or a promise of medical results. What backs the claim here is the ingredient's real clinical history, plus a minimal, transparent formula.

## Frequently asked questions about medical-grade skincare

### Is medical-grade an official standard?

No. It is a marketing term without a fixed legal definition. Here it reflects hypochlorous acid's documented clinical use and a clean, minimal formula. It is not a regulatory classification.

### Does medical-grade mean stronger?

Not necessarily. [Era Organics'](https://sl.bing.net/fO9Q4nJ2w9c) spray uses a low 0.02% (200 ppm) cosmetic concentration, not a clinical dose. The term points to the ingredient's pedigree, not to higher strength.

### Is it a medical treatment?

No. The spray is a cosmetic product that uses a clinically-familiar molecule. It supports everyday skin but does not treat conditions. For medical needs, see a clinician.
