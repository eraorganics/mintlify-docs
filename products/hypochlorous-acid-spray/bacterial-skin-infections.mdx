## What are bacterial skin infections?

Bacterial skin infections are a category of skin infections caused by the proliferation of pathogenic bacteria on or in the skin. As skin infections they sit beside their fungal and viral cousins, but the cause is what defines them: bacteria multiplying faster than the body can control them. The medical literature also calls them cutaneous bacterial infections, or pyoderma when pus is involved. Staphylococcus aureus is the most common culprit. These infections need medical attention, and often antibiotics. Prevention, though, is largely about keeping the surface clean and the barrier intact.

## How clinicians grade a skin bacterial infection

Skin bacterial infections are described along a few practical axes, and those axes decide how urgent the care is. Pathogen load is the count of bacteria present on a given patch of skin, and a heavier load generally means a more entrenched problem. Infection severity sorts the picture into mild, moderate, and severe bands, based on how deep the infection runs and how much tissue it involves.

Duration is the third axis, and it splits infections into acute cases that resolve within a few weeks and chronic ones that persist beyond that. A long duration, a high pathogen load, or rising infection severity all point the same direction: toward a clinician, not a cosmetic. What separates a bacterial case from fungal skin infections or viral skin infections is the organism at the root of it. Fungal skin infections respond to antifungals, viral skin infections to antivirals, and neither responds to the antibiotics that clear a bacterial case — so that distinction changes the whole treatment, which is why diagnosis belongs to a professional rather than a guess at the sink.

## How hypochlorous acid reduces surface bacteria

Hypochlorous acid works by oxidizing bacteria on contact. It reaches the cell walls that microbes depend on and breaks them. ## How to manage bacterial skin infections at the surface

The line here is important. To manage bacterial skin infections at a preventive level — reducing everyday surface bacteria — is not the same as treating an established infection. An active infection with spreading redness, warmth, pus, or fever needs a doctor, and often prescription antibiotics matched to the pathogen load and duration of the case.

### What is pyoderma?

Pyoderma is the clinical term for pus-producing bacterial skin infections, often driven by staph or strep. It is distinct from a fungal skin infection or a viral skin infection, which are caused by entirely different organisms and treated in entirely different ways. Any suspected pyoderma should be seen by a clinician.
