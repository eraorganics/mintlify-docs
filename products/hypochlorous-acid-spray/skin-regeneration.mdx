## What is skin regeneration?

Skin regeneration is a biological process — the body's natural way of replacing damaged or dead skin cells with new ones. It belongs to the same family of biological processes as wound healing, and it runs constantly, speeding up after damage from a wound or a procedure. The process goes by more than one name: dermal regeneration in clinical writing, skin renewal in everyday terms. Whatever the label, the skin regeneration process describes the same cycle of building fresh tissue to replace worn or injured tissue.

Three attributes describe how this biological process unfolds. The first is cell turnover rate — the epidermis renews itself roughly every 28 to 40 days as new cells rise to the surface. The second is collagen synthesis rate, the pace at which the skin lays down structural protein, which varies with age and condition. The third is tissue repair duration, the days to weeks a damaged area takes to rebuild. These figures describe the biology itself, not any product.

Hypochlorous acid does not regrow skin — the skin does that. What the molecule does is help clear obstacles to the process. A 2020 review noted that hypochlorous acid [increases oxygenation at the wound site to improve healing](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31904191/), alongside broad antimicrobial action. ## The attributes of the skin regeneration process

Cell turnover rate is the first attribute worth understanding. Fresh cells form in the deeper layers and migrate upward, replacing the outermost ones on a roughly monthly cycle in healthy adult skin. That cycle is what keeps the surface renewing itself even without injury.

Collagen synthesis rate is the second. Below the surface, the skin rebuilds its structural scaffold, and the speed of that rebuilding shapes how well tissue recovers. It slows with age and is affected by overall skin condition, which is why younger skin often repairs faster than older skin.

Tissue repair duration is the third attribute — the window from days to weeks over which a wound, peel, or laser site rebuilds. A clean, low-inflammation surface tends to support that window rather than prolong it. Research ties hypochlorous acid to this kind of supportive environment: a 2025 study found an [acid-oxidising hypochlorous acid solution reduced Staphylococcus aureus and restored a healthier microbiome in fragile skin wounds](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40803690/), and a 2025 facial laser study reported a [hypochlorous acid mist improved healing and cosmesis after the procedure](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40832716/). ## How a clean surface supports repair

The opposite of regeneration is skin degradation — the tissue breakdown that outpaces repair when bacteria and inflammation dominate. Both keep the immune system busy fighting instead of rebuilding, which can stall dermal regeneration. Lowering that load frees the skin to do its own work of renewal.

This is where a gentle, clean routine helps. Repair matters most right after damage: a wound, a peel, or a laser all trigger a burst of rebuilding, and the surface is vulnerable during it.
