## What are reactive oxygen species?

Reactive oxygen species are a type of reactive molecule built around oxygen, and they are the same things most people know by their common names: free radicals, oxygen radicals, or oxygen free radicals. Whichever name you use, they carry unpaired electrons, which makes them quick to react with nearby cells, proteins, and microbes. That reactivity is not a small trait — the molecular reactivity index of these species runs high, which is exactly why they matter to skin at all. The body makes the same molecule on its own.

This is the part most people miss. Reactive oxygen species are not only a source of damage. The immune system deliberately produces them as a weapon. A 2025 clinical review described [hypochlorous acid as an innate immune factor generated by white blood cells](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41462936/) to fight microbes in the skin.

## Oxidation potential and half-life: why these molecules act fast

What makes a free radical a free radical is measurable. The oxidation potential of reactive oxygen species spans roughly 0.5 to 2.8 volts, which is the pull each species exerts on electrons from whatever it meets. A higher oxidation potential means a harder hit on the target. Paired with that is a very short half-life, running from microseconds to minutes depending on the species. An oxygen radical that lives microseconds does its work close to where it forms and then is gone; one that lasts minutes travels a little farther. Together the high oxidation potential and the brief half-life explain the whole behavior: these molecules react hard, react locally, and do not linger. This is the opposite of what antioxidants and reducing agents do — those donate electrons and neutralize radicals rather than tearing electrons away.

## How does the body use reactive oxygen species?

The body uses reactive oxygen species as a controlled defense, driving reactive oxygen species production on demand. When microbes invade, white blood cells called neutrophils arrive and trigger a burst of oxidants. One of those oxidants is hypochlorous acid, made when the enzyme myeloperoxidase acts on hydrogen peroxide.

Hypochlorous acid is the strongest of these. Research on cultured human skin fibroblasts noted that [hypochlorous acid is the most potent oxidant neutrophils produce in meaningful amounts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10775450/). A 2025 clinical review went further and called it an [integral component of the human innate immune system](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41462936/). ## When reactive oxygen species cause damage

Balance is everything with reactive oxygen species. At the right level they kill microbes. In excess, and over long periods, they harm healthy cells too. That chronic overload is what scientists mean by oxidative stress, and it is why the body keeps antioxidants and reducing agents on hand to reduce reactive oxygen species when they build up past what defense needs.

The damage is measurable. A 1998 study found that sustained exposure to [hypochlorous acid activated the p53 stress response in human skin fibroblasts](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9799559/), a marker of cellular strain. The lesson is about dose, not the molecule itself. A controlled 200 ppm mist is a very different thing from chronic inflammation flooding tissue with oxidants. ## Frequently asked questions about reactive oxygen species

### Are reactive oxygen species good or bad for skin?

Free radicals are both, depending on the amount. At controlled levels they are part of the immune system's defense against microbes. In chronic excess they damage healthy cells, which is called oxidative stress — the point where the body needs to reduce reactive oxygen species with antioxidants.

### What is the difference between reactive oxygen species and antioxidants?

They are opposites. Reactive oxygen species pull electrons from cells, which is why their oxidation potential is high. Antioxidants and reducing agents donate electrons and neutralize those radicals, calming the reaction rather than starting one.
