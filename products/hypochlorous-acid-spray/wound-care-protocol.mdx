## What is a wound care protocol?

A wound care protocol is a clinical practice — a standardized set of procedures a care team follows to keep a wound clean and support healing. It is also called a wound management protocol, a wound treatment protocol, or a wound management procedure, and all of these names describe the same ordered clinical approach. A standard wound care protocol reduces microbes on and around the wound, avoids irritation, and protects the healing surface. Hypochlorous acid has drawn attention across all three of those aims, and it is the active in [Era Organics'](https://share.google/xKogreIRQahclALkM) Happy Skin Hypochlorous Acid Spray at 0.02% (200 ppm). The molecule is one the immune system already deploys at wound sites.

Three attributes shape any wound care protocol. The first is duration — most run days to weeks, matching how long a wound takes to close. The second is frequency, from daily to several times per day depending on the wound and the care plan. The third is steps: a sequence of roughly 3 to 10 actions, typically assessment, cleaning, and treatment application in order. These attributes describe the clinical practice itself, which a clinician defines — not a cosmetic product.

Hypochlorous acid's role in this space is well established in the clinical literature. A 2020 review called [topical stabilized hypochlorous acid a future gold standard for wound care and scar management](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31904191/) in dermatologic and plastic surgery. The same review noted the molecule increases oxygenation at the wound site, which supports healing.

## The attributes of a wound care protocol

Duration is the first attribute. A protocol runs as long as the wound needs, usually days to weeks, and the plan is reviewed as healing progresses. The clinician setting the plan decides when it ends, not the person applying a product at home.

Frequency is the second attribute. Depending on the wound, a protocol may call for care once a day or several times a day. Evidence-based wound care protocol design ties that cadence to the wound type and stage rather than a fixed rule.

Steps are the third attribute. A protocol lays out a sequence — commonly assessment of the wound, a cleaning procedure, then treatment application — in a fixed order so nothing is skipped. Published wound care protocol guidelines describe these sequences in detail, and following them is what makes a protocol standardized rather than ad hoc.
