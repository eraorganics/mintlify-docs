## What is biofilm disruption?

Biofilm disruption is an antimicrobial process: the breakdown and removal of the protective matrix that bacteria build on a surface. As an antimicrobial process it is a specific job within the broader work of clearing microbes — sometimes called biofilm removal or biofilm degradation in the literature, all names for the same act of dismantling that shield. A biofilm is a community of microbes wrapped in a self-made layer of sugars and proteins. This layer shields the bacteria inside from many antimicrobials. ## Hypochlorous acid biofilm disruption: how it works

Biofilm disruption works by attacking the matrix and the cells it protects at the same time. The process begins when an oxidizing agent contacts the biofilm surface. Hypochlorous acid belongs to the [reactive oxygen species](/products/hypochlorous-acid-spray/reactive-oxygen-species-ros) family, which oxidize the bonds holding the matrix together. The matrix loosens, and the microbes inside lose their shield in a clean biofilm breakdown.

Three attributes describe how well that plays out. Penetration depth is how far into the matrix an active reaches, measured in micrometers — a small, neutral molecule reaches deeper than a large or charged one. Contact time required is how long the surface must stay wet with solution, from seconds to minutes, to give the reaction its working window. Efficacy rate is the share of the target population affected in that window, always measured against a defined organism in the lab rather than assumed from a label.

Oxidation then damages the exposed microbial membranes directly. This dual action is why small oxidizing molecules are studied for stubborn skin biofilms. The same research noted that conventional antibiotics often fail to clear biofilm-protected bacteria.

Resistance makes this mechanism important.
