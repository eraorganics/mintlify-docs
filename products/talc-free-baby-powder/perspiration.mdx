## What is perspiration?

Perspiration is the salty fluid that sweat glands release onto the skin, where it collects on the surface and pools in skin folds. It is a body secretion — one of the ordinary fluids the body produces — and it is the everyday form of what most people simply call sweat. You will see the same thing named a few ways: as perspiration, as plain body sweat, or by its older term, sudor. However it is labelled, it is the same warm, damp film that forms on the skin when the body is hot, active, or wrapped up in a diaper or tight clothing.

As a fluid, perspiration has a simple makeup. Its main component parts are water, which is by far the largest share, a small amount of dissolved salts that give sweat its faint taste and tang, and a scattering of trace solutes carried along with it. Three attributes decide how much it affects comfort: volume — how much sweat is produced; composition — the water-and-salt mix left behind as it dries; and evaporation rate — how quickly that film can dry off the surface. When sweat evaporates freely, skin feels fresh. When it cannot, the dampness lingers and comfort drops.

## Why perspiration lingers in skin folds

Sweat is only a problem when it has nowhere to go. On open, exposed skin it evaporates quickly and the surface returns to a dry, cool state — a dry skin surface, the natural antonym of a damp fold, defined by the absence of moisture rather than a clammy film. The trouble starts where two skin surfaces meet and press together. In those [skin fold areas](/products/talc-free-baby-powder/skin-microclimate) — the neck creases of a chubby baby, the inner thighs, the underarms, the diaper region — air cannot circulate and evaporation slows to a crawl. Excess perspiration gets trapped between the surfaces, and the film that would have dried in seconds on an arm sits warm and wet in a fold.

That trapped sweat is what turns a normal amount of moisture into a comfort problem. A warm, closed, humid fold changes the whole feel of the skin, shifting the local [skin surface microclimate](/products/talc-free-baby-powder/skin-microclimate) toward damp and clammy. The connotations here are exactly the ones people want to avoid: dampness, heat, and the low-grade discomfort of skin that never quite dries. Left long enough, prolonged wetness can soften the surface of the skin in a fold — which is simply another reason a dry surface feels so much better.

## How a dry surface changes the way skin feels

The comfort difference between damp and dry skin is immediate and physical. Dry skin in a fold glides; damp skin sticks and rubs. When sweat and perspiration are drawn off and the surface returns to dry, the two sides of a fold stop clinging to each other, friction drops, and the whole area feels lighter and fresher. This is not about doing anything to the skin itself — it is purely about the state of the surface. A dry surface simply feels better than a wet one, and keeping a fold dry is the most direct way to keep it comfortable.

This is where an absorbent powder earns its place. By taking up the dampness a fold would otherwise trap, it keeps the surface dry, reduces the skin-on-skin rubbing that damp skin causes, and leaves the area feeling fresh. The powder does not stop the body from sweating; it simply manages the moisture that reaches the surface so the skin stays comfortable.
