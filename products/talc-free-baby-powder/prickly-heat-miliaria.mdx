## What is prickly heat?

Prickly heat is a heat rash, a cluster of tiny bumps that appears on the skin during hot, humid weather. Doctors call it miliaria, and many parents simply call it a sweat rash. All three names point to the same thing: small, sometimes itchy bumps that show up when the skin gets too warm and sweat has nowhere to go. It is common on babies because their sweat glands are still developing, and it turns up most often on the neck, the shoulders, the chest, and inside the warm skin folds where heat and moisture collect.

The mechanism behind it is straightforward. Sweat is meant to rise to the surface and evaporate, which cools the body. When the surface stays hot and damp, some of the sweat ducts become blocked and the trapped sweat gathers just under the skin, forming the little bumps you can see and feel. Its parts are simple to picture: blocked ducts, trapped sweat, and the small raised spots those two produce. That sweat-duct blockage is what gives the rash its distinctive bump pattern of tiny spots. Because the trigger is heat and trapped moisture, the opposite state — cool dry skin, clear skin without those trapped-sweat bumps — is what tends to feel most comfortable.

## What causes a heat rash to appear

Prickly heat is driven by a few consistent conditions, and understanding them explains why it flares when it does. The first is heat: warm weather, a hot room, or too many layers all raise skin temperature. The second is humidity, which slows evaporation so sweat lingers on the surface instead of drying. The third is the blocked sweat duct itself, which is why the rash pattern is made of small bumps rather than broad redness. Put those together in a warm skin fold and you have the setting where a heat rash is most likely to develop.

Babies are especially prone because their skin is thin and their sweat glands are immature, so ducts block more easily. Tight clothing, plastic-backed coverings, and warm bedding can all add to the problem by holding heat and sweat against the skin. This is why the standard, sensible advice for prickly heat centers on cooling the environment, loosening clothing, and letting skin breathe — moving the skin away from the warm, damp state that lets the rash form in the first place. It is worth noting that prickly heat is a passing discomfort of hot conditions rather than a sign of anything harmful, and it usually settles once the skin cools down.

## How keeping skin dry supports comfort

Because heat rash is tied to warmth and trapped moisture, the everyday steps that help are the ones that keep skin cool and dry. Dress a baby in loose, breathable cotton. Keep rooms well ventilated. Offer cool baths on hot days and pat, rather than rub, the skin dry afterward. And pay attention to the warm creases where sweat pools, since those are the areas most likely to stay damp. Keeping those areas dry and airy is simply about comfort — a dry, cool surface feels better against warm skin than a clammy one.

## When a rash needs more than staying cool

Most heat rash fades on its own once the skin cools and dries. But some rashes in warm, damp areas are not simple heat rash, and some heat rash becomes irritated or does not settle. If a rash is spreading, blistering, weeping, painful, or lasting more than a few days, or if a baby seems unwell or feverish, that is the moment to stop guessing and check with a pediatrician.
