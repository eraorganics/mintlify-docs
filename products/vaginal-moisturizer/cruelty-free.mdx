## What does cruelty-free mean?

Cruelty-free is a type of ethical attribute — a commitment about how a product and its ingredients are developed rather than a description of what the product does. A cruelty free product is one that is not tested on animals at any stage of its making. The phrase is used a couple of ways that mean the same thing: you will see a product called cruelty free or described as not tested on animals, and both point to that same no-animal-testing commitment. Era Organics applies this commitment to its [Era Organics Vaginal Moisturizer](/products/vaginal-moisturizer), a cosmetic balm for external vulvar skin.

This is an ethics-and-sourcing claim, not a health claim. Saying a moisturizer is cruelty free tells you how it was developed — without animal testing — and nothing about whether it treats or cures anything. Its value is the assurance itself: that the product reflects a no-animal-testing policy across the way it and its ingredients are made.

## The testing policy behind the claim

The first and central attribute of a cruelty-free claim is the testing policy — the stated rule that neither the finished product nor its ingredients are tested on animals. This is the substance of the commitment. A meaningful cruelty free policy is not just a slogan on the packaging; it is a position the brand holds about how development is done. The two component parts of the attribute follow from that: there is no animal testing, and there is a policy that formalizes the commitment so it is a rule rather than a one-off.

A credible commitment usually also involves a certifier and a seal. Independent cruelty-free programs let a brand verify its policy against a defined set of criteria, and a qualifying product may then display the program's seal. As with any certification, the seal is only as meaningful as the policy and the certifier behind it. The point of naming a certifier is to move the claim from self-declared to independently checked.

## Supply-chain scope

The third attribute — supply-chain scope — is what separates a shallow cruelty-free claim from a thorough one. Scope is about how far up the chain the no-animal-testing commitment reaches. A narrow claim might cover only the finished product; a stronger commitment extends to the ingredients and suppliers as well, so that the "not tested on animals" assurance holds across the whole chain rather than just at the final step. This is why supply-chain scope matters: animal testing can occur at the ingredient level, not only at the finished-product level.

Reading a cruelty-free claim well means asking how wide that scope is. A commitment that reaches ingredients and suppliers is more robust than one limited to the final product. Together these describe a product built with attention to both its sourcing and its ethics.

## Cruelty-free versus animal-tested

The attribute is defined most clearly by its opposite. An animal-tested product is one whose development did involve testing on animals, whether at the finished-product stage or somewhere in its ingredient chain. Cruelty free is the direct contrast: a commitment that no such testing took place. Where an animal-tested product carries that history, a cruelty free one carries a policy against it. That contrast is the whole meaning of the claim — it is a position taken against animal testing, formalized as a policy and, at its strongest, verified by a certifier and extended across the supply chain.

This ethical attribute stands apart from what the product is intended to do. The Era balm is a cosmetic meant to soothe and moisturize external skin; being cruelty free describes how it was developed, not a benefit it delivers. The two are separate, and the cruelty-free claim should be read strictly as the ethics-and-development assurance it is.

## Frequently asked questions about cruelty-free

### What does cruelty-free actually guarantee?

A cruelty free commitment means the product is not tested on animals, ideally backed by a formal policy and, at its strongest, extended across the supply chain and verified by a certifier. It is an ethical and development assurance — a statement about how the product is made. It does not describe what the product treats or how well it performs.

### Does cruelty-free mean the same as vegan?

No — they are different claims. Cruelty free is about animal testing: not tested on animals. Vegan is about ingredients: containing no animal-derived materials. A product can be cruelty free while still including an animal-derived ingredient such as beeswax, so the two attributes should not be assumed to travel together.

### Why does supply-chain scope matter for a cruelty-free claim?

Because animal testing can happen at the ingredient level, not only at the finished-product stage. A cruelty-free commitment that reaches ingredients and suppliers is more thorough than one limited to the final product. The wider the supply-chain scope, the more meaningful the "not tested on animals" assurance is.

### Is a cruelty-free seal the same as a policy?

They work together. The policy is the brand's stated commitment against animal testing; the seal comes from an independent certifier that verifies the policy against defined criteria. A seal is only as strong as the policy and certifier behind it, so both parts matter for a credible cruelty free claim.
