## What is perimenopause?

Perimenopause is the transitional life stage that leads up to menopause, the years when hormone levels begin to swing before periods stop for good. As a life stage, it is the on-ramp to the same hormonal milestone that menopause completes, which is why it is often called the menopausal transition. Some people simply call it pre-menopause, and the two terms point to the same window. Its defining component parts are cycle irregularity — periods that grow closer together, further apart, heavier, or lighter — and estrogen fluctuation, the up-and-down swings in ovarian hormones that make this stage feel unpredictable.

Perimenopause typically begins in the mid-40s, though its onset age range is broad and can start earlier. It can last anywhere from a couple of years to a decade, and its most reliable feature is symptom variability: two people in the same stage can have very different experiences, and any one person's symptoms can shift month to month. That variability sets perimenopause apart from the stages on either side of it. It is not the settled hormonal floor of post-menopause, and it is not the steady rhythm of the reproductive prime — it is the fluctuating bridge between them.

## How hormone fluctuation affects intimate skin

Estrogen helps keep the delicate skin of the vulva supple and well supplied with moisture. During perimenopause, estrogen does not simply fall in a straight line — it fluctuates — but the overall trend of estrogen decline means that dryness can begin well before periods stop entirely. That is why perimenopausal changes to intimate skin often catch people off guard: the dryness can arrive during the transition rather than waiting for menopause proper. Hormonal skin dryness of this kind is one of the earliest genital signs of the shift.

The changes that accompany reduced estrogen span the whole menopausal transition, not just its endpoint. A position statement on the [genitourinary syndrome of menopause](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32852449/) describes a cluster of genital and urinary changes — including vulvovaginal dryness — that can begin in the perimenopausal years and continue afterward, all tied to declining estrogen. A broader review of [lifetime changes in the vulva and vagina](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16208476/) traces how these tissues shift through each hormonal stage of life, the menopause transition included. Both are background context about the life stage; they describe medical and hormonal physiology, not the effect of any cosmetic.

## Why perimenopause dryness feels different

Because perimenopausal hormones swing rather than settle, the dryness can come and go, which makes it easy to dismiss. One month intimate skin feels normal; the next it feels tight, rough, or easily irritated. This on-and-off quality is a hallmark of the transition. ## Frequently asked questions about perimenopause

### Can vaginal dryness start in perimenopause, before menopause?

Yes. Because estrogen begins to fluctuate and trend downward during perimenopause, dryness can appear during the transition rather than waiting for periods to stop. It may come and go with the hormonal swings. This is general physiology, not a product claim.

### What is the difference between perimenopause and menopause?

Perimenopause is the fluctuating run-up; menopause is the point twelve months after the final period. Dryness can occur in both stages. Our [menopause dryness](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/menopause) page covers the later stage.

### Does a moisturizer treat perimenopause?

No. Perimenopause is a hormonal transition managed by a clinician. An external cosmetic moisturizer is only a comfort measure — it soothes and hydrates the outer vulvar skin and helps relieve the feeling of dryness. It does not treat, cure, or prevent perimenopause or any condition.
