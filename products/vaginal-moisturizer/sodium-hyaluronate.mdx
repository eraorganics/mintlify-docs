## What is sodium hyaluronate?

Sodium hyaluronate is a type of humectant, an ingredient that draws water toward the skin's surface and helps hold it there. It is the sodium salt of hyaluronic acid, which is why formulators and ingredient lists use the two names almost interchangeably. On a label you may also see it written as sodium hyaluronan, as the hyaluronan salt, or simply as the HA salt — all four terms describe the same water-loving molecule. Era Organics uses sodium hyaluronate as a key active in its [Era Organics Vaginal Moisturizer](/products/vaginal-moisturizer), a cosmetic balm made for external vulvar skin, where it works alongside plant oils and butters to help the balm feel moisturizing rather than heavy.

As a water-binding humectant, sodium hyaluronate behaves differently from the fats and waxes that sit beside it in a balm. Its job is not to seal the surface but to attract moisture into the outer skin. That makes it the natural complement to the emollient and occlusive ingredients in the same formula, and it is the reason a hyaluronate-containing product can add a light, cushioned feel of hydration to the skin it is smoothed onto.

## The molecule and its component parts

Chemically, sodium hyaluronate is a long chain built from repeating disaccharide units — pairs of glucuronic acid units and N-acetylglucosamine units linked into a polymer. Attaching a sodium ion to the acidic hyaluronic acid backbone produces the salt form, which is more stable and easier to work with in a finished product than the parent acid. Those glucuronic acid units are what give the molecule its strong affinity for water.

A defining attribute of this hyaluronan salt is its water-binding capacity — it is, in the plainest terms, a water binding humectant. Hyaluronic acid and its salt are known for holding many times their own weight in water, and that ability is the whole reason the ingredient earns a place in a moisturizer. Its molecular weight matters too: the same starting material can be milled into higher-molecular-weight grades that sit on the surface and lower-molecular-weight grades that spread more easily across the skin. In a cosmetic balm the ingredient appears at a low use concentration, blended with other actives rather than dosed as a drug — the point is a gentle contribution to surface moisture, not a clinical intervention.

## How sodium hyaluronate supports vulvar hydration

Because it pulls water into the outer skin, sodium hyaluronate contributes to [vulvar hydration](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/vulvar-hydration) — the addition and retention of water in the surface layers of delicate intimate skin. This is where the ingredient's role in the Era balm is best understood: it is one of several ingredients that together help the skin feel softer and more comfortable, not a stand-alone treatment. Topical skincare containing hyaluronic acid has been studied against placebo, and one controlled study of a [topical hyaluronic acid skincare formulation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23016532/) reported a measurable cosmetic benefit for the treated skin. That background helps explain why the ingredient is so widely used as a topical humectant, though it does not describe the Era product specifically.

The contrast with other ingredient classes makes the point clearer. A drying agent or astringent removes moisture and can leave skin feeling tight; a humectant like sodium hyaluronate does the opposite, working to draw and hold water. And unlike an occlusive — a wax or oil that forms a physical seal — a humectant does not block the surface at all. The two work best as partners: the humectant brings water in, the occlusive helps slow its escape. That pairing is exactly how the sodium hyaluronate in an organic balm is meant to work with the coconut oil, shea butter, and beeswax around it.

## Sodium hyaluronate on intimate skin

Hyaluronic acid has been examined in the vulvovaginal setting as well. A literature review of [hyaluronic acid in vulvar and vaginal administration](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33759670/) surveyed its use as a hydrating agent for intimate comfort, and a separate systematic review looked at [hyaluronic acid for postmenopausal vaginal dryness](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33293236/). These reviews are medical background about hyaluronic acid as a category — many of the products they cover are internal or medical preparations. The Era Organics Vaginal Moisturizer is not one of them: it is an external cosmetic balm, applied only to the outer vulvar skin, and none of the clinical outcomes in those reviews are being attributed to it. They simply show why sodium hyaluronate is a sensible humectant to include when the goal is external moisture and comfort.

## Frequently asked questions about sodium hyaluronate

### Is sodium hyaluronate the same as hyaluronic acid?

They are closely related. Sodium hyaluronate is the sodium salt of hyaluronic acid, so it shares the same water-binding backbone. The salt form is more stable in a finished product, which is why it often appears on labels where you might expect to see plain [hyaluronic acid](/ingredients/hyaluronic-acid). Functionally, both act as humectants that draw water toward the skin.

### What does sodium hyaluronate do in a vulvar balm?

It works as a water-binding humectant, attracting moisture into the surface of the skin so the balm feels hydrating and cushioned. In the Era Organics formula it partners with plant oils and beeswax: the hyaluronate brings water in, and the oils and wax help hold it. It is meant to soothe and moisturize external skin, not to treat any medical condition.

### Is it safe for sensitive intimate skin?

Sodium hyaluronate is a widely used, skin-native type of humectant that is generally well tolerated. As with any new product on delicate skin, patch test first and use only on external vulvar skin. If irritation appears, stop and check with a clinician.

### Does sodium hyaluronate seal the skin like an oil?

No — and that is the point. A humectant draws water in, while an occlusive oil or wax forms a seal on top. Sodium hyaluronate is the humectant half of that pairing. In a well-built balm it is combined with occlusive and emollient ingredients so the water it attracts is less likely to evaporate away.
