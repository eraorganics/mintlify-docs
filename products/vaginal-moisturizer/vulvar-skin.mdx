## What is vulvar skin?

Vulvar skin is the delicate external skin of the vulva that surrounds the vaginal opening. It is a type of skin — genuine keratinized skin on its outer surface — and that single fact explains a great deal about how it should be cared for. The terms vulval skin, external genital skin, and intimate skin all refer to the same tissue. Because it is skin rather than internal mucosa, delicate vulvar skin responds to the same moisture principles as skin elsewhere, but it is thinner and more reactive than most, which is why sensitive vaginal skin needs gentler products than a body lotion.

A useful contrast makes this clearer. The thick skin of the palms and soles — palmar skin — has a heavy, multilayered outer barrier built to withstand friction. External intimate skin is the opposite: thin, supple, and easily affected by rubbing, moisture loss, and irritants. Recognizing vulvar skin as a distinct, delicate type of skin is the starting point for understanding why it deserves careful, external-only care.

## The structure of vulvar skin

As an anatomical structure, vulvar skin has the same broad layout as skin elsewhere: an outer epidermis, a supporting dermis beneath it, and — uniquely here — a mucocutaneous junction where the external keratinized skin meets the moist internal mucosa. That junction is one reason the area is described as transitional and sensitive. A 2011 review of the [morphology and physiological changes of genital skin and mucosa](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21325836/) describes how the vulva is keratinized externally and distinct from the vaginal mucosa, and how it changes with age.

Three attributes define how vulvar skin behaves. Its thinness means less tissue stands between the surface and the nerves and vessels below. Its sensitivity means it reacts readily to friction, fragrance, and harsh cleansers. And its permeability — the outer skin being relatively porous here — means ingredients and irritants both pass through more easily than on tougher skin. A 2006 review of [lifetime changes in the vulva and vagina](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16208476/) traces how this cutaneous tissue shifts across puberty, the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause, underscoring that vulvar skin is dynamic rather than static.

## Vulvar skin as part of the body

Vulvar skin is part of the female external genitalia, and like all skin it has skin barrier function — the ability to hold moisture in and keep irritants out. That barrier is what a gentle moisturizing routine supports. Because the tissue is thin and permeable, the quality of the barrier matters more here than on sturdier skin. Keeping external intimate skin comfortable is largely about protecting and supporting that barrier rather than stripping it.

This skin is also affected by vaginal dryness, a common surface concern in which the outer intimate skin loses moisture and begins to feel tight and rough. ## Why delicate vulvar skin needs gentle care

Because sensitive vaginal skin is thin, permeable, and reactive, the ingredients that touch it matter. Fragrance and harsh additives are common irritants on this tissue, which is the practical reason a fragrance-free approach is usually recommended for external intimate skin. The connotations people rightly associate with this area — delicate and sensitive — are not just descriptive; they are a guide to product choice. Gentle, external-only care that supports the skin barrier is the sensible default, and anything applied here should be patch tested first.

## Frequently asked questions about vulvar skin

### Is vulvar skin the same as vaginal mucosa?

No. Vulvar skin is external, keratinized skin, while the vaginal canal is lined by mucosa. They meet at the mucocutaneous junction. This distinction is why external intimate skin is cared for like delicate skin — moisturized on the surface — rather than treated internally.

### Why is intimate skin so sensitive?

Vulval skin is thin and relatively permeable, so it sits close to nerves and vessels and reacts readily to friction, fragrance, and harsh cleansers. That sensitivity is normal for this tissue and is the reason gentle, fragrance-free products are preferred for external genital skin.

### How is vulvar skin different from skin on the hands?

The thick palmar skin of the hands has a heavy protective barrier built for friction. Delicate vulvar skin is the opposite — thin, supple, and easily irritated. The same moisturizing principles apply, but the products must be far gentler.

### Does vulvar skin change over time?

Yes. Background reviews describe how this tissue shifts across puberty, the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and menopause. Those changes can affect surface moisture and comfort, which is why a supportive external routine is often adjusted through different life stages.
