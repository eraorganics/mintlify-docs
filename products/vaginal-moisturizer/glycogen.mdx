## What is glycogen?

Glycogen is a branched glucose polysaccharide — a large molecule built from many linked sugar units. It is a type of polysaccharide, the class of carbohydrates made of long sugar chains, and it is the body's main way of storing glucose for later use. Because it is stored in animal tissue rather than plants, it is sometimes called animal starch, and because of what it is made from, it is also described plainly as a glucose polymer. All three names — glycogen, animal starch, glucose polymer — point to the same branched carbohydrate.

The contrast that defines glycogen is with the small sugars it is built from. A monosaccharide such as glucose is a single sugar unit — a simple sugar. Glycogen is the opposite: hundreds or thousands of those units joined into a branched network. Where a simple sugar is small and quickly used, the glucose polymer is a compact store that the body breaks back down into simple sugars when it needs them. That relationship — large storage molecule versus small building block — is the heart of what glycogen is.

## The structure of glycogen

Glycogen's molecular structure explains its behavior. Its component parts are glucose units strung into chains, connected at regular branch points that give the molecule a tree-like, highly branched shape. Those branch points are not incidental: they create many endpoints where the molecule can be quickly built up or broken down, which is why glycogen is such an efficient store. A second key attribute is its water solubility. Despite its size, the branched glucose polymer is water-soluble, dispersing in watery surroundings rather than clumping — a property that matters for how a compound behaves in tissue and in a formula.

The third attribute worth understanding is glycogen's microbiome role, and this is where intimate biology enters as background context. Vaginal glycogen is a natural substrate for the resident flora. A 2015 study comparing [glycogen and Lactobacillus levels before and after menopause](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25535963/) found that vaginal epithelial glycogen decreases at menopause and that this is associated with reduced lactobacilli — evidence, offered here purely as education, that glycogen is a substrate the intimate ecosystem depends on.

## Glycogen and intimate biology

How does a stored sugar shape a microbial community? A 2018 review of the [physiologic role of lactobacilli](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29951482/) describes how these bacteria metabolize glycogen breakdown products into lactic acid, which shapes the mildly acidic surface environment. In that sense glycogen substrate sits upstream of the whole system. This makes glycogen part of the story of the [vaginal microbiome](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/vaginal-microbiome) — as a food source that the resident bacteria rely on. This is background biology, describing how the body's own tissue and its microbes interact.

That biology is also tied to hormones. A 2022 review of the [postmenopausal vaginal microbiome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36304005/) describes how estrogen drives epithelial glycogen and how its decline alters the microbiome. Presented as education, this explains why intimate biology shifts across life stages — the amount of glycogen the tissue makes tracks the body's estrogen. None of this is a claim about a topical product; it is the natural science behind the ingredient's name.
