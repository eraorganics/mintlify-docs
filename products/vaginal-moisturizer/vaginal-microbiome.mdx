## What is the vaginal microbiome?

The vaginal microbiome is the community of microorganisms that lives on and around vaginal and vulvar tissue. It is a type of microbiome — one of the many microbial communities the human body hosts — and, like the others, it is a normal and expected part of healthy anatomy rather than something to be scrubbed away. The terms vaginal flora, intimate microbiome, and vaginal microbiota all describe the same community. A 2018 overview of the [vaginal microbiome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30848142/) describes its structure and function as a distinct microbial ecosystem.

What sets this ecosystem apart is captured in its defining attributes. Its species diversity is low compared with, say, the gut — and in this case low diversity is the healthy state. That is because of Lactobacillus dominance: the community is typically ruled by a handful of Lactobacillus species. A 2022 study on [host and microbiome interplay in the vaginal microenvironment](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35837395/) describes exactly this — a low-diversity, Lactobacillus-dominated community. The healthy vaginal flora, in other words, is not a crowded jungle but a well-ordered one.

## How the vaginal microbiome works

The intimate microbiome is made of Lactobacillus species alongside other commensal bacteria, and the metabolites those microbes produce. The relationship between them is what shapes the environment. A 2018 review of the [physiologic role of lactobacilli](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29951482/) describes how these bacteria metabolize the breakdown products of glycogen into lactic acid, and that lactic acid is a large part of why the surface environment tends to stay mildly acidic. This is the background biology behind intimate microbiome balance — a self-sustaining loop of substrate, bacteria, and acid.

A third attribute, pH dependence, follows directly. The acidity the lactobacilli produce is bound up with the community that produces it, so the two move together as background physiology. The vaginal microbiome requires glycogen as a substrate — the branched sugar that feeds those bacteria — and you can read more about that molecule and its role on the [glycogen](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/glycogen) reference.

## What affects the vaginal microbiome

The vaginal microbiome is affected by menopause. As educational background, the drop in estrogen around the menopausal transition reduces the epithelial glycogen that feeds lactobacilli, and the community can shift as a result. A 2022 review of the [postmenopausal vaginal microbiome](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36304005/) describes how estrogen drives epithelial glycogen and how its decline alters the microbiome. This is context about the body's own biology — not a claim that any product changes it.

It is worth being clear about scope. A leave-on cosmetic applied to external skin is not a treatment for the microbiome, and nothing here should be read that way. ## Balance versus imbalance

The antonyms sharpen the picture. A sterile environment is not the goal — a healthy intimate microbiome is populated, not empty. At the other extreme is dysbiosis, a loss of the usual Lactobacillus dominance that shifts the community out of its low-diversity, acidic pattern. Healthy vaginal flora sits between those poles: a stable, low-diversity community whose connotations are balance and health. Understanding that spectrum is what makes intimate microbiome balance an educational topic rather than something a skin product acts on.
