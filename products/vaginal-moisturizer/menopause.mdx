## What is menopause?

Menopause is a natural life stage that marks the end of the menstrual cycle, confirmed once twelve months have passed without a period. As a life stage, it belongs to the same family of hormonal milestones as puberty and pregnancy, but it sits at the opposite end of the reproductive span. Older writing sometimes calls it the change or, in clinical language, the climacteric, and all three names describe the same passage. It is driven by two component parts: a gradual hormonal shift and the depletion of ovarian follicles, so that the ovaries release less and less estrogen over time. That estrogen decline is the thread that connects most of what people notice about menopause, from hot flashes to changes in the skin.

Menopause usually arrives between the ages of about 45 and 55, with the average age of onset around 51, though the timing varies widely from person to person. After it, the post-menopausal stage lasts for the rest of life, so the duration of the changes it brings is permanent rather than temporary. It is not an overnight event: the years of fluctuation that lead up to it are a separate stage, and you can read more about that run-up in our page on [perimenopause dryness](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/perimenopause). The contrast helps frame what menopause is by naming its opposites — it is the far bookend from menarche, the first period, and it closes the reproductive years rather than opening them.

## Estrogen decline and the skin

Estrogen does more than regulate the menstrual cycle. It helps keep skin — including the delicate skin of the vulva — supple, resilient, and well supplied with moisture. When estrogen falls at menopause, the external genital skin can grow thinner, drier, and more easily irritated. Menopausal skin changes of this kind are common and well documented. A long-view review of [lifetime changes in the vulva and vagina](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16208476/) describes how the vulvar and vaginal tissues shift with each hormonal stage of life, including the reduced estrogen of the menopausal transition.

Clinicians group the collection of genital and urinary changes that can accompany reduced estrogen under a single umbrella term. A position statement on the [genitourinary syndrome of menopause](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32852449/) from a menopause society explains that these changes, which include vulvovaginal dryness, are common in the years during and after menopause. A separate overview of [the genitourinary syndrome of menopause](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33534428/) notes that such symptoms may affect a large share of women after menopause and stem from the drop in estrogen. These studies are background context about the life stage — they describe medical and hormonal territory, not the results of any cosmetic product.

## Why vaginal dryness is common at menopause

Because estrogen supports moisture in intimate tissue, its decline is a leading reason that vaginal dryness menopause complaints are so frequent. The vaginal dryness that shows up at this stage is a sensation of tightness and reduced surface moisture in the external skin, and it is one of the most talked-about menopausal changes. A review of the role of vaginal lubrication in menopause management notes that [dryness is common during and after menopause](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26707589/) and that non-hormonal moisturizers and lubricants are recognized comfort options. For a fuller look at the sensation itself, see our page on [vaginal dryness](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/vaginal-dryness).

Reduced estrogen can also affect the acid balance of intimate tissue. The mildly acidic surface environment that helps keep intimate skin comfortable depends in part on estrogen, so menopause can disrupt that pH balance. This is educational background about the life stage; comfort measures for external skin sit alongside it, not in place of medical care.

## Frequently asked questions about menopause and intimate skin

### Why does menopause cause vaginal dryness?

Estrogen helps keep intimate skin moist and supple. As estrogen declines through menopause, the external genital skin often becomes thinner and drier, which is why dryness is such a common menopausal change. This is general physiology, not a product claim.

### Can a moisturizer cure menopause dryness?

No cosmetic can cure or treat menopause, atrophy, or the genitourinary syndrome of menopause — those are medical matters for a clinician. An external moisturizer is a comfort measure: it soothes and hydrates the outer vulvar skin and helps relieve the feeling of dryness on the surface. Persistent or severe symptoms should be discussed with a healthcare provider.

### Is the Era Organics moisturizer used inside the body?

### What is menopause versus perimenopause?

Perimenopause is the transitional run-up when hormones fluctuate; menopause is the point twelve months after the last period. Both can bring dryness, but they are different stages — the earlier one is covered on our [perimenopause dryness](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/perimenopause) page.
