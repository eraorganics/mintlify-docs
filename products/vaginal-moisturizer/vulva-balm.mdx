## What is a vulva balm?

A vulva balm is a balm-textured moisturizer made specifically for external vulvar skin. It is a type of vaginal moisturizer, distinguished by its rich, semi-solid texture rather than by any different purpose — the job is still to soothe and hydrate dry external skin. On ingredient labels and product pages you will see the same thing called a vulva balm, a vulvar balm, an intimate balm, or a vulva salve, and all of those names describe a leave-on balm for the outer intimate skin. Because it is a cosmetic moisturizer, its role is comfort and hydration of the surface skin; it is not a medicine and does not treat any condition.

The word balm signals texture. A vulvar balm for dryness is thicker and more cushioning than a thin lotion or a water gel, which is exactly why some people prefer it for very dry or chafed external skin. This page describes what a vulva balm is, what gives it that texture, and how it compares to lighter formats — so the phrase intimate skin balm actually means something when you shop. For the broader category, see the reference on the leave-on [external vaginal moisturizer](/products/vaginal-moisturizer) this format belongs to.

## What gives a vulva balm its texture

A balm gets its body from its component parts. At its core is usually a wax base that holds the whole formula in a soft, pliable solid, an oil phase of plant oils that carries emollient richness, and a humectant that draws in and holds water at the skin's surface. Together, those three parts — wax base, oil phase, and humectant — are what make a balm feel like a balm rather than a runny lotion. The Era organic vulva balm, for example, is built around plant oils and butters with beeswax to give it structure and a humectant to hold moisture; you can read more about the wax's role in the [beeswax](/ingredients/beeswax) reference.

Two attributes define the format. The first is texture: a balm is rich and cushioning. The second is occlusivity — a balm tends to sit on the skin a little longer and slow moisture loss, which is part of why it suits very dry patches. Its application site is the external vulvar skin, applied as a thin, dime-sized amount to clean, dry skin. Those qualities give a balm its protective, comforting character.

## Vulva balm vs a lotion or water gel

The easiest way to place a balm is against its opposites in format. A water gel is mostly water and feels light and cooling but absorbs and evaporates quickly. A lotion sits in between — lighter than a balm, with more water and less cushioning. A balm is the richest of the three: less water, more wax and oil, more staying power on the skin. None is universally best; the right format depends on how dry the skin is and what feels comfortable. For lightly dry skin, a lotion or gel may be plenty; for rough, chafed, or extra-dry external skin, an intimate skin balm gives more cushioning. If you want the physiology behind why very dry skin benefits from a richer format, see the reference on [delicate vulvar skin](/products/vaginal-moisturizer/vulvar-skin).

Because a vulva balm is a cosmetic, the comparison is about feel and format, not about medical strength. A balm is not stronger medicine than a lotion; it is simply a richer texture. All three formats moisturize; they just do it with different amounts of water and oil. As with any new intimate product, patch testing first is a sensible habit, and anyone with a known allergy to bee products or plant oils should check the ingredient list.

## Frequently asked questions about vulva balm

### What is the difference between a vulva balm and a vulva lotion?

Texture and staying power. A balm is rich and cushioning, with more wax and oil and less water, so it sits on the skin longer. A lotion is lighter, with more water, and absorbs faster. Both are leave-on moisturizers for external skin; the balm simply offers more cushioning, which suits very dry or chafed skin.

### Can I use a vulva balm every day?
