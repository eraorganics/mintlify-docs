## Potential uses providers and naturopaths explore

Methylene blue has a long, documented history in medicine, and a growing body of early research is exploring other potential uses. Some healthcare providers and naturopaths look at that research when they consider methylene blue. The uses below are attributed to clinical and research settings — they describe established medical use and scientific interest, not benefits of this product.

### Established clinical and diagnostic uses

These are well-documented uses of methylene blue in professional medical settings, where it is administered by clinicians:

- **Methemoglobinemia.** Methylene blue is a long-established treatment for methemoglobinemia; it interacts with methemoglobin and the red blood cell's enzyme systems to reduce it back to hemoglobin, and is described in the literature as a generally safe drug at appropriate doses (Clifton et al., American Journal of Therapeutics, 2003).
- **Diagnostic and surgical staining.** Its deep color makes it a useful tissue-staining dye in diagnostic procedures — for example, staining methods used in evaluating oral lesions (Sharma et al., Indian Journal of Dental Research, 2021).
- **Critical-care circulatory support.** In intensive care, methylene blue has been used as a selective inhibitor of the nitric-oxide/cGMP pathway to support blood pressure in distributive and septic shock when standard treatment falls short (Kirov et al., Critical Care Medicine, 2001; Jang et al., Journal of Medical Toxicology, 2013).
- **Antimicrobial photodynamic therapy.** It has been studied as a light-activated antimicrobial adjunct in dentistry, such as in the management of oral lichen planus (Sadaksharam et al., Photodermatology, Photoimmunology & Photomedicine, 2012).

### Potential uses under active research (preliminary)

These are directions researchers and some practitioners are actively exploring. Most of this work is early-stage and comes from laboratory or animal studies, so it describes potential and scientific interest — not established outcomes, and not benefits of this product:

- **Cellular energy (mitochondria).** Methylene blue can act as an electron carrier in the mitochondria and has been studied for its potential to bypass damaged links in the cellular energy (ATP) chain (Lee et al., Hepatology, 2015).
- **Cognitive support.** Methylene blue has been the subject of preclinical and early clinical investigation for cognitive dysfunction and neurodegeneration, including work on tau protein aggregation and perioperative neurocognitive disorders (Howland, Journal of Psychosocial Nursing and Mental Health Services, 2016; Wu et al., Drug Design, Development and Therapy, 2026). This research is preliminary and largely in animal models; it is not evidence that this product improves cognition.
- **Antioxidant (redox) activity.** Methylene blue participates in redox reactions with reactive oxygen species produced during normal metabolism; the ionic fulvic-acid minerals and vitamin C are included as complementary redox-active components.

## Safety — read before any use

Methylene blue is not a gentle botanical; it is a pharmacologically active compound, and it carries real, specific risks. Do not use it without understanding them, and only under professional supervision. Era Organics keeps a fuller rundown in its guide to [methylene blue side effects](https://www.eraorganics.com/blogs/beauty-guide/methylene-blue-side-effects).

- **Serotonin syndrome risk — the most important one.** Methylene blue is a monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI). Combining it with serotonergic medications — SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, and similar drugs — can cause serotonin syndrome, a potentially life-threatening reaction. **Do not use this product if you take any serotonergic medication.**
- **Not for use in pregnancy or breastfeeding.**
- **G6PD deficiency.** Avoid methylene blue if you have glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) deficiency.
- **Only under professional guidance.** Any use should be determined and supervised by a qualified healthcare provider, especially if you take any prescription medication or have a health condition — methylene blue interacts with more drugs than the ones listed here.
- Methylene blue can temporarily tint urine (and sometimes the tongue) blue-green, and it stains fabric and surfaces.

Signs of serotonin syndrome include agitation, confusion, rapid heartbeat, high fever, and muscle rigidity. If they occur, stop and seek emergency medical care immediately.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is methylene blue used for?
In professional medicine, methylene blue is an established treatment for methemoglobinemia and is used as a diagnostic and surgical stain and, in critical care, to support blood pressure in certain kinds of shock. Separately, researchers are exploring its potential in areas like cellular energy and cognition — early work that is not an established outcome.

### What do people explore methylene blue for?
Beyond its established medical uses, some people and practitioners explore methylene blue for its potential role in cellular energy, cognitive support, and antioxidant activity. This is based on preliminary research, much of it in the lab or in animals — it is potential, not proven benefit, and not a benefit of this product.

### Is methylene blue an MAOI?
Yes. Methylene blue acts as a monoamine oxidase inhibitor (MAOI), which is the reason for its most serious drug interaction — the risk of serotonin syndrome with serotonergic medications.

### Can I take methylene blue with an antidepressant (SSRI, SNRI, or MAOI)?
No. Combining methylene blue with serotonergic medications — SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, and similar drugs — can cause serotonin syndrome, a potentially life-threatening reaction. Do not use this product if you take any serotonergic medication.

### Can I take it with Adderall, Vyvanse, or Wellbutrin?
These are not the classic serotonergic antidepressants, but they act on overlapping neurotransmitter systems and can raise the risk when combined with an MAOI like methylene blue. Do not combine them without the explicit guidance of a qualified healthcare provider.

### Can I take methylene blue with coffee?
Coffee is not the concern — the serious interaction is with serotonergic and other neurologically active medications. Review any medications and health conditions with a qualified healthcare provider before any use.

### Is methylene blue safe?
It is a pharmacologically active compound with real, specific risks — chiefly the serotonin-syndrome interaction, plus cautions in pregnancy, nursing, and G6PD deficiency. It is not a casual supplement, and any use should be supervised by a qualified healthcare provider. Read the safety section above in full.

### What are the side effects?
Methylene blue can temporarily tint urine (and sometimes the tongue) blue or green, which is harmless and expected, and it stains fabric and surfaces. More serious effects relate to its drug interactions and the cautions above. See the Era Organics guide to methylene blue side effects for a fuller list.

### Why does methylene blue turn urine blue or green?
Because of its deep color, methylene blue can pass into the urine and temporarily tint it blue-green. This is a normal, harmless effect of the dye itself.
