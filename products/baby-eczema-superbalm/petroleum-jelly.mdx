## What is petroleum jelly?

Petroleum jelly is a chemical compound, a semi-solid mixture of hydrocarbons refined from petroleum, used for generations as a simple occlusive moisturizer. It is sold and known under several names: the brand name Vaseline, the technical term petrolatum, and the older label soft paraffin all describe the same material. It sits on the skin and slows water loss, which is why it is a common eczema and diaper-area staple. A [comprehensive review of petroleum jelly](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37315800/) describes how its occlusive properties make it an effective, low-cost moisturizer, while clearing up several myths about it.

Petrolatum works, but it is a petroleum derivative with no active botanicals or skin-protectant ingredient. That is the difference many families weigh when choosing a daily balm.

## The properties behind petroleum jelly benefits

A few physical traits explain the well-known petroleum jelly benefits. Its melting point is fairly high, roughly in the 36 to 60 degree Celsius range, so it stays put as a soft solid at skin temperature rather than running off. Its viscosity is that of a semi-solid at room temperature, thick and greasy, which is exactly what lets it form a lasting occlusive seal. And it is a blend of hydrocarbons of moderate molecular weight, on the order of 300 to 700 grams per mole, large enough to sit on the surface rather than soak in. Together those properties, a high melting point, a semi-solid viscosity, and a moderate molecular weight, are why a thin film locks moisture in so reliably. Proper petroleum jelly application is simply a thin layer over clean, slightly damp skin.

## Plant oils as an alternative occlusive

Emollient plant oils and butters can seal the skin much as petrolatum does, while adding fatty acids and soothing compounds of their own. Research on preventing infant eczema has compared moisturizer types head to head; a [randomized trial in high-risk neonates](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37478291/) tested different moisturizer regimens, including petroleum jelly, for their effect on atopic dermatitis.

## How petroleum jelly compares to other formats

Petroleum jelly sits at one end of a spectrum of moisturizers. At the other end are a water-based moisturizer, a light lotion or gel that is mostly water and absorbs quickly, and the oil-free formula, a product built to skip greasy occlusives entirely. Those lighter formats feel cleaner and suit oily or acne-prone skin, but they do far less to lock moisture into a dry, cracked barrier. Petroleum jelly is the opposite kind of tool: heavy, greasy, and purely occlusive, doing one job very well. A plant-oil balm lands between them, sealing like an occlusive while adding the botanicals a water-based moisturizer or oil-free formula leaves out. Neither extreme is wrong; they are matched to different skin and different needs.
