## What does non-comedogenic mean?

Non-comedogenic is a skin compatibility attribute: it describes a product formulated so it is unlikely to clog pores and trigger comedones, the blackheads and whiteheads behind acne. The same quality gets described in a handful of ways, a formula might be called pore-friendly, non-acnegenic, or non-pore-clogging, but they all point to the same claim about how the product behaves on the skin. The idea grew out of a need for cover-up products that acne-prone patients could use safely; an early study on [non-comedogenic cosmetics](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/138532/) set out to test ingredients for exactly that purpose.

It is a useful label for acne-prone facial skin. But it is worth being precise about it, because not every gentle or natural product is non-comedogenic, and Era Organics would rather be accurate than overclaim.

## What a non-comedogenic claim actually rests on

A genuine claim about non-comedogenic properties rests on a few specifics rather than a marketing word. The first is pore-blocking potential: ideally a formula whose ingredients carry a low or zero comedogenic rating on the traditional scales used to rank oils and butters. The second is formulation compatibility, meaning the finished product is designed and suited for acne-prone skin, not just gentle in general. The third is a safety profile backed by testing, the reason many pore-friendly products describe themselves as dermatologically tested. A non-comedogenic formula that earns the label is one that clears all three: low-comedogenic ingredients, a build meant for acne-prone skin, and testing to back it up. That precision is exactly why we are careful about where the word does and does not belong.

### What is the difference between non-comedogenic and non-acnegenic?

Both describe products meant not to trigger breakouts on acne-prone skin. Non-comedogenic focuses specifically on not clogging pores or forming comedones, while non-acnegenic is a broader claim about not provoking acne of any kind. In everyday product labeling the two terms overlap heavily and are often used to mean much the same thing.

### Can I use Superbalm on my face?
