## What are natural emollients?

Natural emollients are plant-derived oils and butters that soften skin and slow water loss by filling the gaps between surface cells. They are a class of moisturizing agents, the workhorses of gentle skincare, and they anchor most plant-based eczema balms. You will see the same ingredients described in a few ways on labels and in formulas: as organic emollients, as plant-based emollients, or simply as organic moisturizers. All three names point to the same idea, fats from natural sources doing the softening and sealing work. Emollient therapy is a first-line habit in eczema care; a [randomized clinical trial of emollients to prevent pediatric eczema](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40699587/) tested regular emollient use in infants for its effect on developing atopic dermatitis.

Because they simply replace what dry skin has lost, emollients are safe for daily, long-term use, which is exactly what chronic eczema needs. Era Organics' Baby Eczema Superbalm is built on a base of them.

## What makes an emollient effective

Not all emollients behave the same, and a few measurable traits explain why. The first is lipid content: the proportion and type of fatty acids an oil carries, since those lipids are what actually slot into the skin's surface. The second is viscosity, how thick or thin the emollient is, which decides whether it feels like a light oil or a rich butter and how long it stays on the skin. The third is occlusivity, the degree to which it slows water escaping through the surface. This is why gentle natural emollients are chosen for a barrier-repair balm: an ingredient that is rich in skin-matching fatty acids, thick enough to linger, and occlusive enough to hold water in will do more for a dry, cracked barrier than a thin, quickly absorbed lotion. The right natural emollients restore softness precisely because they score well on all three.

## How emollients calm the dry-skin cycle

Eczema-prone skin is caught in a loop: a leaky barrier lets water out, the skin dries and itches, scratching damages it further. Emollients break that loop by resealing the surface and easing the dryness that drives the itch. Guidance on [infant skin care](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31188166/) underscores gentle, regular moisturizing as core to keeping developing skin healthy.

## Natural versus synthetic emollients

Natural emollients stand in contrast to synthetic emollients and chemical moisturizers, which are lab-made ingredients such as mineral oil, silicones, or petrolatum used to fill the same softening role. Both kinds can slow water loss, but they get there differently. Synthetic emollients are inert occlusives that sit on top of the skin; plant-based emollients bring their own fatty acids and soothing compounds into the mix, replacing lipids the skin recognizes rather than only capping the surface. That is the choice behind a plant-based balm: leaning on organic moisturizers instead of the chemical moisturizers that dominate many conventional creams. Neither is inherently unsafe, but for families who prefer to keep petroleum and synthetics off a baby's skin, natural emollients are the point of the formula.

### Are natural emollients good for eczema?

Emollients are a first-line part of eczema care, resealing the barrier and easing dryness and itch. Superbalm is built on plant-derived emollients alongside a colloidal oatmeal active. For moderate to severe eczema, follow a clinician's plan.

### How are natural emollients different from synthetic ones?

Natural emollients are plant-derived oils and butters that add back skin-matching fatty acids, while synthetic emollients such as mineral oil or silicones mainly cap the surface. Both slow water loss; the difference is what they leave behind on the skin.

### How often should I apply an emollient?

Emollients work best applied regularly, often several times a day and after bathing while skin is still damp. Superbalm is made for that kind of daily use.
