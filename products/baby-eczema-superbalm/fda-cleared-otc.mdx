## What does FDA-cleared OTC mean?

FDA-cleared OTC is a form of regulatory approval: it means a product is a recognized over-the-counter drug, not just a cosmetic. The terms FDA-approved OTC and FDA-authorized OTC are used the same way in everyday language, all pointing to a product the U.S. Food and Drug Administration recognizes for a stated use and sale without a prescription. It works under an FDA monograph, a public standard that sets an approved active ingredient, use, and labeling.

Era Organics' Baby Eczema Superbalm qualifies as an FDA-cleared OTC treatment because its active, colloidal oatmeal at 1%, is an FDA-recognized skin protectant. That status is why the balm can state it treats eczema, where a cosmetic cannot. The difference is legal, not marketing. A cosmetic may only "soothe" or "moisturize"; an OTC drug with a monograph active may state a treatment use within its label. Superbalm sits in the second group.

## The attributes that define OTC drug status

This certification comes down to a few concrete attributes. The first is regulatory approval status, which is essentially binary: a product is either recognized under the monograph system or it is not, and only the recognized ones may make a drug claim. The second is over-the-counter availability, meaning the product is sold without a prescription and can be picked off a shelf. The third is documentation, including the FDA clearance number or monograph reference that ties the product's active to an approved standard. Together these make the status verifiable rather than a slogan.

Where an FDA-cleared OTC product sits is clearest by contrast with what it is not. It is not a prescription-only medication, which requires a clinician's authorization and pharmacy dispensing. And it is not an unregulated product making claims with no oversight behind them. It falls in the middle: reviewed against a public standard, yet freely available. That middle ground is exactly why families can reach for an FDA-cleared OTC treatment for eczema without a doctor's script while still getting a labeled drug.
