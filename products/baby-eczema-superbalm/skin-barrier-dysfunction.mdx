## What is skin barrier dysfunction?

Skin barrier dysfunction is a dermatological condition in which the skin's outer defensive layer, the stratum corneum, can no longer hold water in or keep irritants out. It is also called an impaired skin barrier, a compromised skin barrier, or simply barrier impairment, and those names all point to the same problem. It sits at the center of eczema. A review of [filaggrin and the skin barrier in atopic dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37758055/) explains how the outermost layer normally resists environmental threats and maintains balance, and how that resilience fails in eczema-prone skin.

Researchers describe the condition through a few measurable attributes. One is the transepidermal water loss rate, the speed at which water escapes through the skin, which climbs well above the roughly 25 grams per square metre per hour seen in resilient skin when the barrier leaks. Another is the stratum corneum pH levels at the surface: a healthy stratum corneum stays mildly acidic, and in a compromised skin barrier the pH drifts upward past about 5.5, which favours irritation and microbes. A third is the lipid bilayer organization integrity, the state of the tightly packed fatty layers between skin cells, which drops when the barrier is disrupted so the mortar between skin cells no longer holds. When the barrier leaks, water escapes and allergens and microbes get in, which sparks the inflammation and itch of a flare. Each of those measures moves in the wrong direction at once in eczema-prone skin, which is why a single flare can feel like several problems layered together.

## The genetics and mechanics of chronic skin barrier dysfunction

Part of the story behind chronic skin barrier dysfunction is genetic. A review of [atopic dermatitis pathophysiology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/38724781/) describes how loss-of-function mutations in filaggrin raise water loss through the skin, shift its pH, and leave it dehydrated, all hallmarks of a dysfunctional barrier. Filaggrin helps knit the outer cells together and generate the natural moisturizing factors that keep the surface hydrated, so when it is short, the lipid matrix loosens and corneocyte cohesion weakens.

The opposite of all this is what care aims for: an intact skin barrier with healthy skin barrier function, low water loss, a mildly acidic surface, and well-ordered lipids. That is why care for eczema-prone skin leans so heavily on rebuilding the surface rather than only fighting inflammation. Restoring lipids and water supports [barrier function improvement](/products/baby-eczema-superbalm/barrier-function-improvement), and it is the reasoning behind emollient-rich balms for daily use.
