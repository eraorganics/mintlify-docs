## What is hydrocortisone?

Hydrocortisone is a type of topical corticosteroid, the most common over-the-counter steroid for calming eczema flares. It is the synthetic form of cortisol, the hormone the body makes naturally, and in a pharmacy setting it is sometimes abbreviated HC. As a corticosteroid, hydrocortisone works by suppressing inflammation in the skin, which reduces redness and itch. Its low strength makes it the mildest of the prescription and non-prescription steroids, and applied as a cream it is a familiar first step for a flare.

Its trade-off is well documented. A comprehensive review of [topical corticosteroid-induced skin atrophy](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25862024/) describes thinning of the skin as an established adverse effect of repeated steroid use, one reason these creams are meant for limited courses rather than indefinite daily application.

## The attributes of a mild corticosteroid

A few attributes describe how hydrocortisone behaves. Its molecular weight is modest, in the range of a small steroid molecule, which helps it penetrate the outer skin. Its anti-inflammatory potency sits at the mild end of the corticosteroid scale, below the medium and high-potency steroids a dermatologist might prescribe for stubborn disease. And its topical concentration is typically low, with over-the-counter products at the bottom of the usual range and stronger versions available only by prescription. Those three attributes together explain why hydrocortisone is effective quickly yet reserved for short courses.

By its nature, a corticosteroid is the opposite of an immunostimulant or a pro-inflammatory agent. Where those would ramp the skin's inflammatory response up, hydrocortisone damps it down. That is precisely why it relieves an angry flare, and also why long, uninterrupted use can leave skin thinner than before. Whether you meet it as a topical hydrocortisone cream or a hydrocortisone ointment, the same calculus applies: strong on a flare, but on a clock.
