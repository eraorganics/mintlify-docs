## What is seborrheic dermatitis?

Seborrheic dermatitis is a common form of dermatitis that causes greasy, flaky scales in oil-rich areas of the skin. It is a chronic, recurrent inflammatory skin condition, meaning it tends to come and go over time rather than clear once and stay gone. The name has a few variants: it is also written seborrhoeic eczema, shortened to seborrhea, and, when it appears on an infant's scalp, called cradle cap. An [overview of seborrheic dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16848386/) describes it as scaling of the scalp and central face, with scales that are greasy rather than dry, and flares that stress can trigger.

A few attributes help define it. Its affected body regions are the oil-rich zones, chiefly the scalp, the face around the eyebrows and nose, and the chest. Its prevalence is modest, affecting roughly one to three percent of the population. And its chronicity is the recurrent, on-and-off pattern noted above. Those features together separate it from a passing rash and mark it as a long-running tendency of the skin. In babies, the same condition on the scalp is the familiar [cradle cap](/conditions/cradle-cap). A review of [infantile seborrheic dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16776285/) notes it as a self-limiting condition of early infancy that usually clears on its own.

## How it differs from eczema

Seborrheic dermatitis and eczema can look similar but behave differently. Seborrheic dermatitis is greasy and often not very itchy, while eczema is dry, intensely itchy, and inflamed. Care differs too: medicated seborrheic dermatitis often responds to antifungal shampoos, while eczema care leans on moisturizing and a skin protectant. The goal in either case is to move flaky, inflamed skin back toward healthy, moisturized skin.

Seborrheic eczema is not the same as ordinary dandruff either, though the two overlap. Dandruff is flaking without much inflammation; seborrhea adds redness and greasy scale. It also differs from psoriasis, which produces thicker, silvery plaques on a wider range of body sites, and from a simple contact rash that clears once the trigger is removed. Recognising which condition is present matters, because the antifungal shampoos that help seborrheic dermatitis do little for eczema, and vice versa. That said, gentle emollient care overlaps for both, softening scales and calming redness. Plant oils like [coconut oil](/ingredients/coconut-oil) help lift greasy flakes without harsh scrubbing, loosening scale so it can be combed or wiped away rather than picked at, which only inflames the skin further.

## Frequently asked questions about seborrheic dermatitis

### Is seborrheic dermatitis the same as eczema?

They are related but distinct. Seborrheic dermatitis, or seborrhoeic eczema, is greasy and scaly and often not very itchy; eczema is dry, itchy, and inflamed. Both are forms of dermatitis. Superbalm's active is labeled for eczema, while on mild seborrheic dermatitis the balm is used to gently soften and soothe.

### Can I use Superbalm on seborrheic dermatitis?

Superbalm can help soften scales and calm irritation on mild, everyday seborrheic dermatitis and cradle cap. Patch test first, and see a clinician if it is widespread, weepy, or not improving.

### What triggers seborrheic dermatitis flares?

Stress, cold dry weather, and oily skin can all set off flares on the scalp, face, or chest. Because seborrhea is chronic and recurrent, gentle, consistent skin care between flares helps keep the skin comfortable and closer to its moisturized baseline.
