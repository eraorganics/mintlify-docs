## What is seborrheic dermatitis?

Seborrheic dermatitis is a common, mild skin condition that causes greasy, flaky scales in oil-rich areas like the scalp, eyebrows, and sides of the nose. An [overview of seborrheic dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16848386/) describes it as scaling of the scalp and central face, with scales that are greasy rather than dry, and flares that stress can trigger.

In babies, the same condition on the scalp is known as [cradle cap](/conditions/cradle-cap). A review of [infantile seborrheic dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16776285/) notes it as a self-limiting condition of early infancy that usually clears on its own.

## How it differs from eczema

Seborrheic dermatitis and eczema can look similar but behave differently. Seborrheic dermatitis is greasy and often not very itchy, while eczema is dry, intensely itchy, and inflamed. Care differs too: medicated seborrheic dermatitis often responds to antifungal shampoos, while eczema care leans on moisturizing and a skin protectant.

That said, gentle emollient care overlaps for both, softening scales and calming redness. Plant oils like [coconut oil](/ingredients/coconut-oil) help lift greasy flakes without harsh scrubbing.

## Frequently asked questions about seborrheic dermatitis

### Is seborrheic dermatitis the same as eczema?

They are related but distinct. Seborrheic dermatitis is greasy and scaly and often not very itchy; eczema is dry, itchy, and inflamed. Superbalm's active is labeled for eczema, while on mild seborrheic dermatitis the balm is used to gently soften and soothe.

### Can I use Superbalm on seborrheic dermatitis?

Superbalm can help soften scales and calm irritation on mild, everyday seborrheic dermatitis and cradle cap. Patch test first, and see a clinician if it is widespread, weepy, or not improving.

### What triggers seborrheic dermatitis flares?

Stress, cold dry weather, and oily skin can all set off flares. Gentle, consistent skin care between flares helps keep the skin comfortable.
