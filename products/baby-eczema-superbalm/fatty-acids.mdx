## What are fatty acids in skincare?

Fatty acids are a type of lipid, the building-block fats found in plant oils, and they are part of what keeps the skin barrier intact and holding water. Chemically, each fatty acid is a carboxylic acid with a long hydrocarbon chain, which is why the singular term fatty acid and the plural lipid acids both point at the same family of molecules. In a balm, they are the components that give an oil its richness and its ability to sit on and sink into the skin.

Linoleic acid, an essential omega-6 fatty acid, is especially important to healthy skin. A review of [linoleic acid in skin and hair health](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39796110/) describes how it is incorporated into the complex lipids of the skin barrier and supports its function. Because the body cannot make essential fatty acids, they have to come from the diet or, topically, from the oils in a balm. That is one reason plant-oil formulas matter for eczema-prone skin.

## The attributes that make omega fatty acids useful

A few physical attributes explain why these lipid acids behave the way they do on skin. Chain length varies across the family, running from short four-carbon acids up to long twenty-eight-carbon chains, and the longer, richer chains are the ones that feel emollient and occlusive in a balm. Saturation level matters too: saturated fatty acids are firmer and more stable, while unsaturated ones, like the omega fatty acids in sunflower and avocado oil, stay fluid and are the more active barrier lipids. Those differences also drive melting point, which spans a wide range and is why some oils are liquid at room temperature and others, like coconut oil, are semi-solid.

What fatty acids are not is just as telling. They are not synthetic polymers or lab-built film-formers, and they are not mineral compounds like petrolatum that only sit on the surface. A leaky, eczema-prone barrier is often short on the very lipids fatty acids help supply, so replenishing them with the same kind of molecule the skin already uses supports genuine repair rather than a passive seal.

The mix of fatty acids matters as much as the amount. A blend that pairs the lighter, unsaturated omega fatty acids of sunflower and avocado oil with the firmer, saturated fatty acids of coconut oil and beeswax gives a balm both quick absorption and lasting occlusion. The unsaturated acids sink in and feed the barrier's own lipid layers; the saturated ones stay nearer the surface and slow water loss. Choosing oils for their fatty-acid profile, rather than for scent or texture alone, is why a plant-oil balm can rebuild the barrier instead of only sitting on top of it. That is also why these lipids belong to a different category than the synthetic polymers and mineral compounds that only form a passive film.
