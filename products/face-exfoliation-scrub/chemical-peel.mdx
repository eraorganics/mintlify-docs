## What is a chemical peel?

A chemical peel is an exfoliation treatment that applies acids to dissolve the outer layers of skin so a fresher surface can come through. It is a type of exfoliation treatment that works entirely by chemical exfoliation — the acid loosens and removes cells with no scrubbing at all — which is what separates it from a physical scrub. Depending on the setting you will hear it called an acid peel, a facial peel, or simply a skin peel, and all three describe the same idea of resurfacing the skin with a controlled acid application. Peels range from mild at-home formulas to strong professional procedures, and that range in strength is the single most important thing to understand about them.

This puts a chemical peel at the potent, resurfacing end of the exfoliation shelf, and it is a genuinely valuable treatment when the goal is intensive renewal. It is also the natural antonym of a physical scrub or an enzyme mask. A physical scrub buffs cells away by friction; an enzyme mask digests them gently with a fruit protein; a peel dissolves them with acid. None of these is inherently better — they are different tools for different goals — but a peel asks the most of the skin, which is why it comes with the most care. A gentle scrub and a strong peel are not substitutes for each other; they occupy different places in a routine.

## Acid strength, depth, and contact time

Three attributes decide what a chemical peel does: acid strength, depth, and contact time. Strength is the concentration and type of acid, and it spans a wide range — a light at-home glycolic formula is worlds apart from a high-strength clinical peel. Depth follows from strength: a superficial peel works only on the very outermost layer, while deeper peels reach further and are strictly professional procedures. Contact time is how long the acid stays on the skin before it is neutralized, and it interacts with the other two. Because these levers combine, "chemical peel" covers everything from a mild weekly refresh to a serious medical treatment.

The working parts of a peel are straightforward: the acid solution itself, a neutralizer to stop the acid at the right moment, and an applicator to spread it evenly. Stronger peels are monitored by the professional performing them, while gentle at-home versions are formulated to be self-limiting. Whatever the strength, a peel calls for a patch test first and careful aftercare, because more powerful acids ask more of the skin. A mild at-home [glycolic acid peel](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/glycolic-acid-peel) sits at the gentle end of this spectrum, and even that benefits from the same sensible precautions.

## Chemical peel versus a gentle scrub

A chemical peel and a physical scrub both exfoliate, but they are different tools for different goals and are not meant to be used together on the same day. A peel resurfaces intensely by dissolving layers of cells; a scrub gently buffs the loose surface film away by friction. If your aim is a strong resurfacing effect, a peel is the right choice — ideally with a patch test and, for anything beyond a mild formula, professional guidance. Some routines make room for both, keeping them on separate days so the skin is never over-worked, and always following either with hydration. ## Frequently asked questions about chemical peel

### What is the difference between a chemical peel and a face scrub?

A chemical peel dissolves layers of dead cells with acid and no scrubbing, while a face scrub buffs the surface film away by friction from fine particles. A peel is a resurfacing treatment that can be quite intensive; a scrub is a gentle cosmetic product for everyday smoothing. They are different tools for different goals, not substitutes.

### Can I use a face scrub and a chemical peel on the same day?

No — both exfoliate, and using them together can over-work the skin. Keep a peel and a physical scrub on separate days, and moisturize after either. If you use both, space them out so the surface has time to recover between them.

### Do I need a patch test before a chemical peel?

Yes. A patch test on a small, discreet area first helps you check how your skin responds before applying an acid to the whole face. This matters most for stronger peels, and anything beyond a mild at-home formula is best done with professional guidance. Aftercare and sun protection are part of using any peel.

### Is a chemical peel better than a scrub?

Neither is universally better; they do different jobs. A peel resurfaces intensely by dissolving cells, while a scrub gently polishes the surface by friction. For a strong resurfacing effect, a peel is the tool; for a gentle, regular smoothing habit, a scrub is. The right choice depends on your goal and your skin.
