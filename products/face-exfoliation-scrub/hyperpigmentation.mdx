## What is hyperpigmentation?

Hyperpigmentation is a type of skin discoloration in which patches of skin look darker than the surrounding area. The common names describe what you see: dark spots, uneven skin tone, or post-inflammatory marks left behind after a blemish or irritation heals. Under a microscope the cause is extra melanin — the brown pigment that gives skin its colour — deposited unevenly, so some areas carry more pigment than others.

The three things people notice are darkness (how deep the colour is), distribution (whether it sits in scattered spots or broad patches), and the overall melanin content of the affected skin. Sun exposure, hormones, and the aftermath of inflammation are typical triggers for excess melanin. The opposite state — an even skin tone, a uniform complexion — is simply skin where pigment is spread evenly across the surface. Hyperpigmentation is best thought of as a colour concern, which is different from the surface-roughness concern of [uneven skin texture](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/uneven-texture), even though the two often appear together and both fall under the umbrella of an uneven-looking complexion.

## Why pigment settles unevenly

Melanin is produced by cells deep in the skin and carried upward as new skin cells rise toward the surface. Some of that pigment ends up in the outer layers, where it becomes visible as dark spots and uneven tone. When pigment production ramps up — after sun, after a breakout, after irritation — those deposits concentrate in patches. Because a portion of pigmented cells sits in the outermost layers that the skin naturally sheds, surface renewal is one of the levers that influences how even the tone looks over time. This is an appearance matter, not a claim that any product controls melanin from the inside.

## How gentle exfoliation supports a more even-looking tone

Since some pigment is carried in the dead cells at the surface, gently and regularly removing that surface layer can help skin look more even in tone over time. It does not lighten skin, treat hyperpigmentation, fade dark spots as a drug would, or prevent discoloration. ## Using a scrub safely around pigmented skin

Aggressive scrubbing is especially counterproductive with pigmentation, because irritation can trigger more melanin and leave new post-inflammatory marks. Keep it gentle: two to three times a week, once for reactive skin, light pressure, a short circular massage, then rinse and apply a [face moisturizer](/products/face-moisturizer). Daytime sun protection matters here more than anywhere, since sun exposure deepens existing dark spots. Skip exfoliation on active breakouts, sunburned, or post-procedure skin, and patch test first because the scrub contains walnut shell, a tree nut.

See a clinician for hyperpigmentation that is spreading, new, or changing in shape or colour — a professional can distinguish ordinary sun and post-inflammatory marks from pigmentation that needs medical attention, and can recommend treatments a cosmetic routine cannot provide.

## Frequently asked questions about hyperpigmentation

### Does a face scrub fade dark spots?

No. A scrub is a cosmetic and does not fade, lighten, or treat dark spots. Gentle regular exfoliation can help the complexion look more even over time by buffing away surface dead cells, but that is an appearance-level effect only, not treatment.

### Can exfoliating even out skin tone?

It can support a more even-looking tone by removing surface dead cells that carry some pigment, so skin looks fresher and more uniform. The effect is gradual and cosmetic. For discoloration itself, leave-on acids and clinician-guided options are the real tools.

### Will scrubbing make hyperpigmentation worse?

Aggressive scrubbing can, because irritation can trigger more melanin and cause new post-inflammatory marks. Keep exfoliation gentle and infrequent, protect skin from the sun, and see a clinician if spots are changing.

### What actually helps hyperpigmentation?

Sun protection is foundational. Leave-on acids like AHAs and clinician-guided treatments are designed to work on pigment. A gentle scrub is a supporting, appearance-level step — not a treatment for the discoloration itself.
