## What is acne?

Acne is a type of skin disorder — an inflammatory condition of the hair follicles and their oil glands. In plain terms it is what most people call breakouts, blemishes, or pimples. It is a medical condition, not just a cosmetic concern, and that distinction matters for everything that follows on this page: a face scrub is a cosmetic and cannot treat, cure, or prevent it. What a scrub can do is help skin look smoother between breakouts. Understanding acne first makes it clear where a gentle exfoliation habit belongs and, just as importantly, where it does not.

Acne shows up in a range of lesion types and severities. A 2015 overview of [acne vulgaris](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27189872/) and a 2025 review that set out to be a [decoding of acne vulgaris pathogenesis and management](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40195701/) both describe the same core parts: comedones (the clogged pores, both open and closed), papules (small raised bumps), and pustules (the inflamed, pus-topped spots). Inflammation level and severity vary from a few occasional bumps to widespread, painful lesions. The opposite state people want is clear skin, or blemish-free skin — and the frustration of acne-prone skin is a real emotional weight, not a vanity problem.

## What drives a breakout

Acne begins where congestion begins. When excess dead cells and oil collect inside a follicle, they form a plug — the process called comedogenesis, described in a 2003 review of [comedogenesis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12566800/). That plug is the seed of a comedone. From there, trapped sebum and bacteria can drive the redness and swelling that turn a quiet comedone into an inflamed papule or pustule. So the same skin oil that leaves pores looking congested is one of the players in a breakout — you can read more about the underlying oiliness on the topic of excess sebum. The clogged-pore stage is covered in more depth under [clogged pores on face](/concepts/congested-pores), and the darkened open comedones many people picture are blackheads, discussed as plain text here rather than linked.

## Where gentle exfoliation fits — and where it does not

Here is the careful part. A scrub is a cosmetic. It does not treat acne, clear acne, or prevent acne, and no scrub should ever be presented that way. What gentle, regular exfoliation between breakouts can do is buff away the loose dead cells sitting on the surface, so skin looks smoother and pores look clearer. That is an appearance-level benefit only. The [dual action face scrub](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub) from Era Organics pairs micronized rounded walnut-shell powder with papaya enzyme to lift and dissolve that surface buildup gently, which is why it belongs in the calm periods between breakouts rather than in the middle of them.

The hard rule for active acne: do not scrub over active pustules or inflamed breakouts. Dragging abrasive particles across an inflamed lesion can rupture it and spread bacteria across the skin, making things worse. On acne-prone skin during a flare, a scrub is the wrong tool. The ingredient built to work inside a congested follicle is a leave-on chemical exfoliant such as [salicylic acid](/ingredients/salicylic-acid), which is a common feature of over-the-counter acne care — but even that sits alongside, not inside, a physical scrub.

Most importantly: acne is a medical condition, so see a clinician for acne treatment. A dermatologist can match the right treatment to your lesion type and severity — something no cosmetic scrub can do. Use exfoliation only as a gentle, between-breakouts habit for the look and feel of your skin, and let a professional handle the acne itself.

## Using a scrub safely on acne-prone skin

If your skin is acne-prone but currently calm, keep exfoliation gentle and infrequent — two to three times a week at most, once for reactive skin, with light pressure and a short circular massage. Rinse and follow with a [face moisturizer](/products/face-moisturizer) to keep the barrier comfortable. Stop immediately if skin becomes red or irritated. Skip exfoliation entirely during an active breakout, on sunburned skin, or after any procedure. ## Frequently asked questions about acne

### Does a face scrub treat acne?

No. A face scrub is a cosmetic, not a treatment. It cannot treat, cure, or prevent acne. Gentle exfoliation between breakouts can help skin look smoother and pores look clearer, but that is an appearance-level effect only. See a clinician for acne treatment.

### Can I scrub a pimple to get rid of it?

No — this is the thing to avoid. Scrubbing over active pustules or inflamed breakouts can rupture them and spread bacteria, which worsens acne. Keep abrasive scrubs away from active lesions and use them only when skin is calm.

### When should I exfoliate if I have acne-prone skin?

Only between breakouts, when skin is calm, and gently — two to three times a week at most. During a flare, skip the scrub. And because acne is a medical condition, work with a clinician on the treatment side rather than relying on exfoliation.

### What actually helps with acne?

A clinician can prescribe or recommend treatments matched to your acne. Over-the-counter routines often include a follicle-active ingredient like salicylic acid. A scrub is not a treatment — it is a cosmetic step for the look of skin between breakouts.
