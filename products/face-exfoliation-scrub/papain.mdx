## What is papain?

Papain is a proteolytic enzyme extracted from the papaya fruit (Carica papaya) and used in skincare to dissolve the protein bonds that hold dead skin cells together. In the language of biochemistry, papain is a type of proteolytic enzyme — a protein-digesting catalyst — which sets it apart from the acids used in chemical peels and the abrasive granules used in physical scrubs. On ingredient lists and in product copy it goes by several names: you will see it as papaya enzyme, as papaya proteinase, or by its source, Carica papaya enzyme, but all refer to the same fruit-derived protease. Era Organics uses papain as the enzymatic phase of its dual-action Revive+ scrub, where it works after the physical grit has buffed the surface.

Like all enzymes, papain is itself a protein with working parts. Its component pieces include a folded polypeptide chain that forms the body of the molecule, an active site where the actual chemistry happens, and — at the heart of that site — a reactive cysteine residue that does the cutting. That cysteine is why papain is classed as a cysteine protease. Those parts together make papain a tool for a specific job: cleaving the bonds inside keratin.

## How papain digests keratin

Dead skin cells are held in place by protein "rivets" — bundles of keratin and the desmosome links between cells. Papain's proteolytic activity works by cleaving the peptide bonds in those proteins, loosening the glue so the dead cells can lift away. This is the core of enzyme exfoliation: instead of grinding cells off with friction or burning through the bonds with acid, the enzyme quietly digests the protein that anchors them. Because it breaks down keratin, papain removes the keratinized debris that dulls the surface and roughens texture. This bond-cleaving action is the mechanism behind [enzyme exfoliation](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/enzyme-exfoliation), the gentler cousin of acid resurfacing.

Three attributes define how a papain exfoliant behaves. Its source is the papaya fruit, a tropical origin that gives it its natural, plant-derived character. Its proteolytic activity — how vigorously it digests protein — determines how much lifting it does. And it is pH sensitive: papain works best within a particular acidity range and slows outside it, which is part of why a well-formulated scrub is buffered to keep the enzyme in its active zone. Together these make papain a precise, gentle exfoliant rather than a blunt one.

## Why papain is selective for dead cells

The most useful property of a papaya enzyme is its selectivity. Living skin cells are not left defenseless against a protein-digesting enzyme — they carry natural protease inhibitors, such as alpha-1-antitrypsin, that block papain from attacking healthy tissue. Dead corneocytes on the surface have lost that protection. The practical result is selective action: the enzyme concentrates on the keratin of dead cells while living cells below are shielded. That built-in selectivity is what makes a papaya enzyme mask or scrub feel so gentle compared with a strong acid, which cannot tell living tissue from dead.

This selectivity is why papain suits skin that reacts badly to harsher methods. It contrasts with two antonyms. Against a synthetic acid, papain is a natural protein-digester that leans on the skin's own inhibitor system for restraint rather than sheer concentration. Against an abrasive granule, papain does its work chemically, with no friction at all — no scrubbing required. Enzyme exfoliation of this kind belongs to the broader category of chemical exfoliation, which loosens dead cells by dissolving their bonds rather than sanding them away.

## Frequently asked questions about papain

### What does papain do for your skin?

Papain is a papaya-derived enzyme that dissolves the keratin bonds holding dead skin cells to the surface. By loosening that glue, it lifts away dull, flaking debris and helps skin look smoother and more refined. It exfoliates chemically, without any scrubbing.

### Is papain gentler than acid exfoliation?

For many people it feels gentler. Papain is selective — living cells are protected by natural protease inhibitors, so the enzyme concentrates on dead cells. A strong acid cannot make that distinction. This selectivity is why enzyme exfoliation often suits skin that reacts to acid peels.

### Is papain natural?

Yes. Papain is a proteolytic enzyme extracted from the papaya fruit, Carica papaya. It is the same enzyme sometimes used to tenderize meat, valued in skincare for the same protein-digesting action, applied gently to loosen dead surface cells.

### Can papain be used on sensitive skin?

Its selective, no-friction action makes a papaya enzyme a popular choice for reactive skin. Even so, patch test any new product first and start with occasional use. Enzyme exfoliation is not meant to be a daily habit, and freshly exfoliated skin needs sun protection the next morning.
