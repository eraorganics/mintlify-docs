## What is a glycolic acid peel?

A glycolic acid peel is a chemical exfoliating treatment that uses glycolic acid to loosen and dissolve the bonds between surface skin cells. It is a type of chemical peel — the family of acid-based treatments that resurface skin without any scrubbing — and glycolic is the smallest of the alpha hydroxy acids, which is why this kind of peel is also called an AHA peel, a glycolic peel, or simply an acid peel. All of those names point to the same idea: instead of buffing cells away by friction, the acid weakens the glue holding them together so the tired surface layer sheds more readily. It is part of the wider category of chemical exfoliation, and it comes in gentle at-home strengths as well as stronger professional formulas.

This is the antonym of a physical scrub or an enzyme mask. A physical scrub lifts cells by touch; an enzyme mask digests them with a fruit protein; a glycolic acid peel dissolves the bonds with an acid you cannot feel working the way you feel a scrub. None of these is inherently better than another — they are different tools for different goals. A peel is prized for its potency and its precise, resurfacing character, and it earns a real place in many routines. The Era Organics scrub simply sits at the gentler end of the same shelf, pairing physical and enzyme action rather than relying on acid.

## Acid concentration, pH, and contact time

Three attributes decide how a glycolic peel behaves: the acid concentration, the pH, and the contact time. Concentration is how much glycolic acid the formula holds — at-home glycolic peel products tend to sit lower, while professional peels go considerably higher. The pH matters just as much, because a lower, more acidic pH makes the same concentration act more strongly. Contact time is the third lever: how long the acid stays on the skin before it is rinsed or neutralized. Because these three interact, a well-formulated peel is not just "more acid is stronger" — it is a balance of all three, and stronger peels are the ones that most need care.

The working parts of a peel reflect this. There is the glycolic acid itself, usually a buffer that keeps the pH in a controlled range, and often a neutralizer to stop the acid's action at the right moment. A professional peel is monitored by the person applying it; an at-home version is formulated to be gentler and self-limiting. Whichever you use, the acid works on the same target the friction methods do — the outer layer of dead cells — just by a chemical route. That shared goal is why a peel and a scrub can complement each other in a routine, provided they are not stacked on the same day.

## Using a peel and a scrub on different days

A glycolic acid peel and a physical scrub both exfoliate, and using both at once is too much for the skin. The sensible approach is to keep them on separate days: an acid peel resurfaces chemically, a scrub buffs mechanically, and giving the skin a rest between them prevents over-doing it. Many people alternate — a gentle scrub a couple of times a week, a peel on a different day — so the surface is renewed without being overworked. Whichever you have used, follow it by replenishing the skin with a [face moisturizer](/products/face-moisturizer) and add sunscreen the next morning, since freshly exfoliated skin benefits from sun protection.

## Frequently asked questions about glycolic acid peel

### Can I use a glycolic acid peel and a face scrub on the same day?

It is best not to. Both a glycolic acid peel and a physical scrub exfoliate, and combining them in one session can leave the skin over-worked. Space them out on different days and moisturize afterward. Alternating a gentle scrub and an occasional peel lets each renew the surface without doing too much at once.

### What is the difference between a glycolic peel and a face scrub?

A glycolic acid peel dissolves the bonds between dead cells with an acid, while a face scrub lifts them away by friction from fine particles. One is chemical exfoliation, the other physical. They reach the same dead-cell layer by different routes, and many routines use both on separate days for a balanced approach.

### Is a stronger glycolic peel always better?

Not necessarily. A peel's effect depends on acid concentration, pH, and contact time together, and higher strength also asks more of the skin. Lower-strength at-home peels are gentler and self-limiting, while high-strength peels are usually professional treatments. The right strength depends on your skin and your goal, not on potency alone.

### Do I need to moisturize after a glycolic acid peel?

Yes. Like any exfoliation, a peel clears the surface layer, so following it with a moisturizer restores hydration and comfort. Sun protection the next day is also worthwhile, since newly resurfaced skin is more exposed. The same aftercare applies whether you exfoliated with an acid, an enzyme, or a scrub.
