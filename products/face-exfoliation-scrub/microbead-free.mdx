## What does microbead-free mean?

Microbead-free means a scrub uses biodegradable, naturally sourced grit to buff the skin instead of the tiny plastic beads that older exfoliants relied on. In the language of cosmetic formulation, microbead-free is a type of formulation attribute — a statement about what a product leaves out and what it uses in place of it. The same idea appears on labels under a few names — as a plastic-free exfoliant, as no microbeads, or as a biodegradable scrub — but each describes the same choice: natural abrasive grit rather than synthetic polymer spheres. Era Organics built the physical phase of its Revive+ scrub on plant-derived grit, so it exfoliates without any plastic beads.

The attribute breaks down into three component parts. There is the natural abrasive itself — the grit that does the buffing. There is the plain absence of plastic beads, no polyethylene spheres anywhere in the formula. And there is the biodegradable grit that replaces them, material that breaks down in the environment rather than persisting. Those three together are what let a scrub deliver the same tactile smoothing without the synthetic microplastic that gave the old scrubs their texture.

## What plastic microbeads were, and why they were phased out

Plastic microbeads were tiny, perfectly round spheres of polyethylene or polypropylene added to scrubs, cleansers, and toothpastes to provide cheap, uniform abrasion. Their problem was not on the skin but afterward. Because they are small, solid, and non-biodegradable, they slip through wastewater filters and end up in rivers, lakes, and oceans, where they accumulate and are eaten by aquatic life. That environmental impact led many places to restrict or ban plastic microbeads in rinse-off cosmetics. The bead that felt smooth in the shower turned out to be a persistent piece of microplastic downstream.

This is the antonym at the heart of the attribute. Polyethylene beads and plastic microbeads are exactly what a microbead-free scrub is defined against. Removing them is not a cosmetic downgrade — a well-milled natural grit buffs the surface just as smoothly — it is an environmental upgrade, swapping a persistent synthetic for a material that breaks down.

## How biodegradable grit replaces the beads

A plastic-free exfoliant leans on natural abrasives to do the physical work. In the Revive+ formula that grit is [micronized walnut shell](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/walnut-shell-powder), ground fine and rounded so it lifts loose cells by friction the way a bead once did — but as a plant-derived, biodegradable material. Because the grit is milled into rounded, uniform granules, it delivers the smoothness of a bead without the sharp edges of a crude crush, and it does so with [micronized particles](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/micronized-particles) rather than plastic. When the scrub washes down the drain, the natural grit breaks down instead of joining the microplastic load in waterways.

Three attributes define a good microbead-free design. The abrasive source should be natural and renewable rather than petroleum-derived plastic. The biodegradability should be genuine, meaning the grit breaks down after use. And the overall environmental impact should be lower across the product's life, from sourcing to rinse-off. A biodegradable face scrub that meets all three gives you the eco-friendly, responsible option without asking you to give up the buff. That physical grit is only one part of the wider [dual action formula](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/dual-action-formula), where an enzyme handles the deeper exfoliating work.

Choosing a microbead free scrub is one of the simpler environmental swaps in a skincare routine, because the alternative is so clearly worse. Plastic beads offered no benefit that natural grit cannot match, and they carried a lasting cost. A plant-based abrasive closes that gap: the same immediate smoothing, none of the downstream microplastic. It is the responsible, natural version of a physical scrub. ## Frequently asked questions about microbead-free scrubs

### Why are plastic microbeads a problem?

Plastic microbeads are tiny, non-biodegradable spheres that slip through water treatment and accumulate in rivers and oceans as microplastic, where aquatic life ingests them. That environmental harm led many places to restrict them in rinse-off products. A microbead-free scrub avoids the problem entirely by using biodegradable natural grit.

### What replaces microbeads in a natural scrub?

Naturally sourced abrasives do the buffing instead. In the Revive+ formula that is micronized walnut shell — plant-derived grit milled into rounded, uniform granules. It lifts dead cells by friction the way a bead did, but it is biodegradable and plastic-free.

### Does microbead-free mean less effective exfoliation?

No. A well-milled natural grit buffs the surface just as smoothly as a plastic bead did. Microbead-free changes the material, not the result. If anything, rounded natural grit paired with an enzyme lets the scrub exfoliate thoroughly at lower pressure.

### Is a biodegradable scrub better for the environment?

Yes, in the sense that its abrasive breaks down after use instead of persisting as microplastic. Swapping plastic beads for biodegradable grit removes a lasting downstream cost while keeping the same smoothing action, which is why it is considered the more responsible choice.
