## What anti-inflammatory properties describe

Anti-inflammatory properties are a biological activity: the ability of a substance to calm the skin's inflammatory response, the familiar redness, heat, and swelling that follows injury, irritation, or microbial challenge. Inflammation itself is a normal defense. When skin is disturbed, immune cells move into the area and release chemical messengers that widen blood vessels and recruit more cells, which is what produces the visible flush and swelling. A substance with anti-inflammatory activity works on those signals rather than masking the appearance — it helps quiet the messengers that keep the reaction going, so the tissue can settle. This is the property being described when researchers talk about calming, soothing, or comforting inflamed skin.

Two well-studied examples of this property show up in skincare science: hypochlorous acid, a molecule the body's own immune cells make, and colloidal oatmeal, a long-used botanical ingredient. Both have been examined for how they act on the inflammatory response, and looking at each explains what "anti-inflammatory properties" actually means at the skin surface.

## Hypochlorous acid and the inflammatory response

Hypochlorous acid is not a foreign chemical to the skin; it is an innate immune factor the body produces itself. A 2025 clinical review described [hypochlorous acid as an innate immune factor with anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and immunomodulatory properties](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41462936/), spanning uses across dermatology and wound care. The review credited that combination of activities — calming the inflammatory signaling while also acting on microbes — for the molecule's role in supporting skin as it settles.

That property has been examined in the context of in-office procedures, where inflammation runs high by design. Laser resurfacing, peels, and similar treatments leave skin red and reactive for days as it heals. A 2025 study found that a [hypochlorous acid mist improved healing and cosmetic outcomes on the face after laser treatment](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40832716/), with the authors tying the benefit to the molecule's combined anti-inflammatory and antimicrobial action. Because hypochlorous acid is small, uncharged, and gentle, it can be applied as a fine mist with no rubbing — useful on skin that is temporarily fragile. This overlaps with the molecule's [antimicrobial activity for skin](/products/hypochlorous-acid-spray/antimicrobial-activity), the same surface action from a different angle.

## Colloidal oatmeal and its soothing chemistry

Colloidal oatmeal is the other side of this topic — a plant-derived ingredient with a long clinical history of soothing irritated skin. Its activity comes from its chemistry. A 2015 study characterized the [anti-inflammatory activities of colloidal oatmeal](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25607907/), attributing them in large part to a group of compounds called avenanthramides, which act on the signaling behind redness and itch. Beyond those compounds, oat also contains starches and beta-glucans that hold water and form a soft protective film on the surface.

A review of [colloidal oatmeal's history, chemistry, and clinical soothing properties](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17373175/) traces its long use for dry, itchy, and irritated skin, and explains why it became a standard soothing ingredient: it works on both the inflammatory signaling and the physical comfort of the surface at once. Colloidal oatmeal is the recognized skin-protectant active in many balms and creams for exactly this reason, and its comforting effect on the skin surface connects directly to [barrier function improvement](/products/baby-eczema-superbalm/barrier-function-improvement) on dry, easily disturbed skin.

## Frequently asked questions about anti-inflammatory properties

### What does "anti-inflammatory" mean for skin?

It describes a substance's ability to calm the skin's inflammatory response — the redness, heat, and swelling that come with irritation. Rather than covering up the look, an ingredient with this property acts on the immune signaling behind the reaction, helping the skin settle. Ingredients described this way are common in care aimed at sensitive, reactive, and irritated skin.

### Does hypochlorous acid have anti-inflammatory properties?

It has been studied for them. A 2025 clinical review described hypochlorous acid as an innate immune factor with anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and immunomodulatory properties, and a 2025 study reported better healing and cosmetic outcomes when a hypochlorous acid mist was used on the face after laser treatment. It is a molecule the body's own immune cells already produce.

### Why is colloidal oatmeal considered soothing?

Colloidal oatmeal contains compounds called avenanthramides that have been studied for anti-inflammatory activity, along with starches and beta-glucans that hold water and form a soft film on the skin. Together these give it its long-recognized ability to comfort dry, itchy, and irritated skin, which is why it is a standard skin-protectant ingredient.

### Are these ingredients right for post-procedure or very reactive skin?

Both have been examined in gentle, fragrance-free contexts suited to easily disturbed skin, and hypochlorous acid specifically has been studied in post-procedure care. For a specific procedure, always follow the aftercare instructions from the clinic that performed it, and see a clinician for any skin condition that is not settling.
