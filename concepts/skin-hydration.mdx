## What keeps skin hydrated

Skin hydration is a property of the skin's outermost layer, the stratum corneum: it is the amount of water that layer holds and keeps from evaporating. A well-hydrated surface stays soft, supple, and comfortable, while a depleted one turns tight, rough, and flaky. The water is not simply sitting on top of the skin; it is bound inside the corneum by a mix of small, water-attracting molecules collectively called the natural moisturizing factor, along with structured lipids that slow evaporation. When researchers describe the [molecular mechanisms of skin hydration](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17524122/), they point to this interplay between water-binding compounds and the lipid matrix that surrounds the skin cells.

Two forces govern how much water the surface retains. The first is the skin's own supply of humectants — glycerol, urea, amino acids, and lactate among them — that pull water into the corneum and hold it there. The second is the barrier of lipids that caps the surface and limits how fast that water escapes. Hydration is high when both are working: plenty of water drawn in, and little of it lost. When either falters, the surface dries out, and dryness itself makes the barrier leakier, which lets even more water go. This self-reinforcing loop is why dry skin can feel like it never fully recovers on its own.

## Humectants and occlusives: two ways to raise hydration

Topical products raise skin hydration through two complementary mechanisms, and most well-formulated moisturizers use both. Humectants are water-loving ingredients — hyaluronic acid, glycerin, sodium hyaluronate — that attract and bind water into the corneum, mimicking the skin's own natural moisturizing factor. Occlusives are oils, waxes, and butters that sit on the surface and form a breathable layer, slowing the rate at which water evaporates away. A review of [topical emollients and moisturizers in dry skin](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14572299/) describes how pairing humectants with occlusives breaks the dry-skin cycle: the humectant restores water, and the occlusive keeps it from leaving before the barrier has time to recover.

This is more than a comfort question. Hydration and the skin barrier are tightly linked. Work on [moisturization and skin barrier function](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14728698/) shows that raising the water content of the corneum also supports the barrier itself — better-hydrated skin holds together better and defends more effectively against irritants. And in skin that has already dried out or become compromised, straightforward emollients do real work: research on [restoring skin hydration and barrier function](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40231699/) with basic emollients found that simple, well-chosen moisturizers restore both the water level and the barrier at once. Hydration, in other words, is not a cosmetic afterthought layered on top of skin health — it is part of the machinery of it.

## Frequently asked questions about skin hydration

### What is the difference between hydrating and moisturizing?

In everyday use the two overlap, but they describe different actions. Hydrating means adding water to the skin, usually with a humectant that binds moisture into the corneum. Moisturizing more often refers to sealing that water in with an occlusive or emollient so it does not evaporate. Skin that is well cared for gets both: water in, and a layer over the top to keep it there.

### Can water alone hydrate the skin?

Plain water on the surface evaporates quickly and can leave skin feeling tighter than before. Lasting hydration depends on water-binding molecules that hold moisture in the corneum and on a surface layer that slows its escape. That is why a mist or a rinse feels refreshing for a moment, while a formulated moisturizer keeps the surface comfortable for far longer.

### Why does my skin get drier in winter?

Cold air holds less moisture, and indoor heating dries it further, so the environment pulls water out of the skin faster than usual. At the same time, low humidity means the surface has less to draw from. The result is a corneum that loses water quickly and struggles to replace it — the same dry-skin cycle, accelerated by the season. Humectant-and-occlusive care helps counter it.
