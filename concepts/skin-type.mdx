## What is a skin type?

A skin type is a categorization of skin by traits such as oiliness, dryness, and sensitivity. It is a type of dermatological category — a practical way of sorting complexions so that a routine can be matched to what a face actually needs. You will see it called a few different things: some people say skin classification, others say skin category, and beauty writing often uses complexion type. They all mean the same thing, and knowing yours is the first step to choosing an exfoliator for your skin type rather than guessing.

The whole point of the idea is that skin is not uniform. The opposite assumption — a universal skin that behaves the same for everyone, a one-size-fits-all approach — sets people up to over-treat one face and under-treat another. Sorting skin into types replaces that with something personal: a suitable rhythm and pressure for each surface.

## The traits that define a skin type

Three attributes do most of the sorting: oil level, sensitivity level, and hydration level. Oiliness is how much natural oil the surface carries — a shiny T-zone versus a matte, sometimes tight feel. Sensitivity is how readily the skin reacts, whether it flushes, stings, or stays calm when something new is applied. Hydration level is the water content of the surface, which shows up as plumpness and comfort rather than shine. Read together, those three traits place a complexion into a familiar category, and each category wants a slightly different exfoliation frequency.

## The main skin types and how often to exfoliate

Normal, or balanced, skin has even oil and moisture and generally tolerates regular gentle exfoliation. For this type, exfoliating 2 to 3 times a week is a comfortable middle ground that keeps the surface looking smooth without pushing it.

Oily skin produces more surface oil, so it often looks shiny and can hold onto dead-cell buildup around pores. It usually handles the upper end of that range — around 3 times a week — well, since lifting the flakes that mix with oil helps keep the surface looking clearer.

Dry skin carries less oil, can feel tight, and shows flakiness sooner. It does better with lighter, less frequent exfoliation — once or twice a week — followed by a [nourishing face moisturizer](/products/face-moisturizer), so the surface is smoothed without being left feeling stripped.

Combination skin, or combo skin, is oily through the T-zone and drier on the cheeks, so it has mixed needs across one face. A middle rhythm of about 2 times a week works for most of the face, with a lighter touch on the drier cheeks and a little more attention to the oilier center.

Sensitive skin, sometimes called reactive skin, flushes and reacts easily and has the lowest tolerance. It wants the gentlest approach: about once a week, light pressure, and a soothing follow-up. If skin is reactive, a low-friction [enzyme exfoliation](/products/face-exfoliation-scrub/enzyme-exfoliation) is usually kinder than aggressive scrubbing.

## Frequently asked questions about skin type

### How do I know my skin type?

Look at three things a few hours after washing: how oily the surface feels, how easily it reacts to products, and whether it feels hydrated or tight. A shiny all-over face leans oily, a tight flaky one leans dry, a mixed face is combination, and a face that flushes or stings easily is sensitive.

### Does skin type change how often I should exfoliate?

Yes. Exfoliation frequency is one of the main things skin type decides. Normal and oily skin often suit 2 to 3 times a week, combination skin around twice a week with a lighter touch on the cheeks, dry skin once or twice, and sensitive skin about once a week with gentle pressure.

### Can one scrub work for different skin types?

A gentle, dual-action scrub can, because you adjust the frequency and pressure to your complexion type rather than the formula. Oilier skin can exfoliate a little more often, while drier and more reactive skin uses it less frequently and follows with moisturizer.

### What if I am not sure of my skin type?

Start conservatively: patch test, then exfoliate once a week with light pressure and see how the skin looks and feels. You can always build up gradually. Going slowly is the safe way to learn what your skin category tolerates.
