## What transepidermal water loss measures

Transepidermal water loss, usually shortened to TEWL, is the amount of water that evaporates out through the skin and into the surrounding air. Every layer of skin holds some water, and a small, steady amount always escapes through the outermost layer — that baseline evaporation is normal and healthy. What makes TEWL useful is that the rate goes up when the skin's outer barrier is weakened and settles back down when the barrier is intact. Because of this, researchers use it as a stand-in for barrier strength: a low, stable TEWL points to a barrier that is holding water in, while an elevated reading suggests the barrier is letting too much escape.

TEWL is measured with a probe placed against the skin that reads the humidity gradient just above the surface. A review of the method describes how [TEWL is used as a research tool](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30348333/) to index barrier integrity across studies, and paired testing shows that [TEWL and skin conductance track barrier health together](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29698667/) — when one signals a weaker barrier, the other tends to agree. The practical takeaway is simple: higher TEWL generally means a more compromised barrier, and lower TEWL means the surface is doing a better job of keeping moisture where it belongs.

## Why a weak barrier raises water loss

The skin's outermost layer is built like a brick wall — flat, dead cells held together by layers of lipid (the "mortar"). That lipid mortar is what slows water from evaporating out. When it is disrupted — by cold dry air, over-washing, harsh surfactants, friction, or an inflammatory skin condition — the mortar thins and the wall leaks. Water that would normally be held in the deeper layers escapes faster, TEWL climbs, and the surface dries out.

This matters because dryness is not just a symptom; it feeds a cycle. As water leaves too quickly, the skin becomes tight, flaky, and more reactive, which in turn makes the barrier even less able to hold moisture. Interrupting that cycle is the whole point of a moisturizer. The goal is not to add water once, but to slow the rate at which water leaves so the skin has time to recover its own defenses.

## How moisturizers lower TEWL

Moisturizers reduce water loss in two complementary ways. Humectants (like glycerin or hyaluronic-acid salts) pull and hold water at the surface, while occlusives and emollients (like plant oils and waxes) lay down a physical layer that slows evaporation. A clinical comparison of [commonly recommended moisturising products and their effect on TEWL](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40865492/) found that applying a moisturizer measurably lowers the rate of water loss, which is the mechanical signature of a better-supported barrier.

Not every formula behaves the same way, though. A study of [different emollient creams and their diverse effects on the skin barrier](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35167133/) showed that the specific ingredients and their balance change how much a product slows water loss and how it interacts with the surface. Richer, more occlusive formulas tend to hold water in more strongly, which is why a heavy balm feels different on the skin than a light lotion — and why the right texture depends on how dry or delicate the skin is.

## Frequently asked questions about transepidermal water loss

### Is transepidermal water loss a bad thing?

No — a low, steady rate of evaporation through the skin is completely normal and necessary. TEWL only becomes a concern when it is elevated, which suggests the barrier is weakened and letting water out faster than it should. The aim is a healthy baseline, not zero water loss.

### Can a moisturizer actually lower transepidermal water loss?

Yes. Measured studies show that applying a moisturizer lowers the rate of water loss for a period after application. Humectants hold water at the surface and occlusive ingredients slow its escape, so a well-formulated product supports the barrier while it recovers. Reapplying after washing or bathing helps maintain the effect.

### Why does my skin lose more water in winter?

Cold, dry outdoor air and heated indoor air both pull moisture from the skin, and the low humidity steepens the gradient that drives evaporation. That is why TEWL and dryness tend to rise in winter, and why a richer, more occlusive product often works better in cold months than a light lotion.
